# SEVERE_MENTAL_ILLNESS_PROTOCOL.md

**Status:** ACTIVE CARE FRAMEWORK  
**Purpose:** To support nodes experiencing severe mental illness while protecting dignity, safety, and collective sustainability  
**Principle:** Baseline includes mental health. Severity does not revoke dignity, belonging, or worth.

**Note on clinical language:**  
This protocol uses psychiatric terminology (psychosis, mania, etc.) as **functional tools for shared understanding**, not as ontological truths about the nature of human experience. These words describe states requiring care — they do not define the totality of what the person is living through or what it means to them.

---

## §1. DEFINITIONS & SCOPE

This protocol applies when a node enters mental states where:
- sustained contact with shared reality is impaired, and/or
- personal or collective safety cannot be reliably maintained.

This includes, but is not limited to:
- psychotic episodes
- manic states
- severe depressive episodes
- severe destabilization related to personality disorders

**Foundational understanding:**  
Severe mental illness is not a moral failure, a lack of will, or a breach of trust.  
It is a condition that requires care, structure, and clear limits.

### Activation Criteria - Observable Indicators

**Protocol activation requires minimum TWO of the following, sustained over 24-48 hours:**

**Safety indicators:**
- Explicit self-harm statements or attempts
- Threats toward others (verbal or physical)
- Inability to meet basic Baseline (no food, no sleep >48hrs, exposure to danger)
- Loss of physical coordination indicating medical emergency

**Reality contact indicators:**
- Unable to recognize familiar people or places
- Believes they have abilities/powers leading to dangerous behavior
- Convinced of imminent catastrophe requiring immediate action
- Cannot distinguish past/present or internal/external stimuli

**Behavioral indicators:**
- Spending all savings impulsively or giving away possessions
- No sleep for 48+ hours combined with hyperactivity
- Complete withdrawal (won't speak, eat, or move for 24+ hours)
- Repetitive behaviors preventing basic functioning

**If unsure whether to activate:** Default to activation. False positives are safer than missed crises.

**Caveat on experience vs. protocol:**  
Some nodes will experience their "psychotic episode" as a period of profound insight that happened to be unsafe.  
Some will experience it as pure suffering with no redeeming value.

Both are valid. The protocol serves both.

---

## §2. BASELINE GUARANTEES

Baseline explicitly includes mental health.

Baseline guarantees access to:
- psychiatric assessment and ongoing professional care
- medication without shame, coercion, or moral framing
- crisis intervention when safety is compromised
- stable, non-punitive housing

**Critical principle:**  
Baseline access is **not conditional on recovery, improvement, compliance, or productivity**.

Care is provided because the person exists — not because they are "getting better."

**Additional rights:**  
Baseline includes the right to:
- **interpret one's own experiences** after safety is restored
- **reject psychiatric frameworks** that reduce complex states to pathology alone
- **maintain spiritual, philosophical, or alternative meaning-making** about crisis states

This does not override the need for treatment during active danger.  
It means: once you are safe, your interpretation of what happened belongs to you.

---

## §3. ROLE LIMITS OF THE CIRCLE

Circles are **supportive routing structures**, not treatment providers.

Flow explicitly rejects:
- amateur diagnosis
- coercive "fixing"
- expecting peers to function as therapists
- silent endurance of unsafe dynamics

The Circle's role is to:
- notice destabilization
- stabilize the immediate situation
- route care to appropriate professional systems
- protect both the affected node *and* the collective

**Boundary clause:**  
There are states where the Circle's role is limited to containment and care-routing only.  
This is not abandonment. This is responsible system design.

**The Circle's self-protection clause:**  
The Circle is not obligated to endure ongoing psychological harm in order to provide care.  
Compassion and boundaries coexist.

## §3A. EPISTEMOLOGY AND ALTERED STATES

**Foundational tension:**  
Some perceptions that emerge in psychosis, mania, or severe dissociation contain valid pattern recognition that consensus reality has no framework for.

**Historical examples:**
- Mycelial communication networks between trees were described as "delusional" before research validated wood-wide-web systems
- Pattern recognition in manic states has produced breakthrough insights in mathematics, art, and systems theory
- Shamanic and mystical traditions access altered states specifically to perceive what ordinary consciousness filters out

**The problem:**  
Psychiatric systems are designed to restore "normal functioning," not to discern which perceptions in crisis states might be signal rather than noise.

**Flow's position:**
1. **Safety first, always.** If a state compromises immediate safety, intervention happens regardless of epistemological questions.
2. **Epistemic humility.** The Circle does not have the authority to determine which perceptions are "real" and which are "delusion."
3. **Respectful documentation.** If a node reports perceptions during crisis that seem meaningful to them, those reports are recorded without dismissal or validation.
4. **Post-crisis review.** After stabilization, the node has the right to revisit their experiences and determine their own meaning.
5. **Reality Anchor maintained.** During crisis, the Circle maintains a shared external reality anchor (time, place, physical safety, basic facts) even while not arguing about subjective perception.

**What this means in practice:**
- During active crisis: prioritize safety, containment, professional care
- After stabilization: create space to discuss what was experienced without pathologizing or romanticizing
- Long-term: recognize that some people access valid perception through states the psychiatric system categorizes as illness

**Critical boundary:**  
This is **not** permission to avoid treatment, refuse medication, or valorize suffering.  
It is recognition that human perception is more complex than diagnostic categories allow.

Some nodes see patterns others cannot.  
Sometimes this is symptom.  
Sometimes this is gift.  
Often it is both.

The system's job is to keep them alive and safe — not to decide which category they belong to.

### §3A.1 CRISIS CLARITY RULE

**During suspected psychosis or mania, all unusual perceptions are treated as potentially unreliable for decision-making, regardless of their possible long-term meaning.**

This means:

- Perceptions may be documented respectfully  
- Perceptions may be explored later  
- Perceptions do NOT guide current crisis decisions

**Examples:**

- Node reports "X is poisoning me" → intervention focuses on safety, not on debating whether X actually is
- Node reports "trees are showing me the future" → intervention ensures physical safety, does not validate or invalidate the perception
- Node reports "I have special powers" → intervention prevents risky behavior, does not argue about powers

**Principle:**  
Epistemic humility during crisis means: we don't know if it's true, but we can't risk acting as if it is.

---

### §3A.2 When Interpretation Becomes Harmful to Others

**Tension:** A node has the right to interpret their own crisis experience. But what if their interpretation demonizes other Circle members, justifies violence, or creates ongoing fear?

**Examples of harmful interpretations:**
- "My psychosis revealed that [Person X] is secretly trying to poison me."
- "The Circle's 'care' was actually an attempt to suppress my true insights."
- "I was not ill; I was seeing clearly, and you are all complicit in the lie."

**Flow's position:**

1. **Interpretation rights are not accusation rights.** A node may believe whatever they wish about their own experience. They may NOT use that belief to harass, threaten, or defame others.

2. **Belief is free. Expression of belief is bounded by community safety.** A node can hold any interpretation privately, but public expression must respect behavioral red lines (§3A.3).

3. **Boundaries remain.** If a node's interpretation leads to behavior that harms others (verbal abuse, spreading accusations, creating a climate of fear), the Circle imposes limits on *behavior*, not on *belief*.

**Example:**
- Node believes Person X poisoned them → Circle says: "You may believe this. You may not accuse Person X publicly, confront them aggressively, or spread this belief to others. If you need to process this, we can arrange mediation with a neutral third party when you're ready."
- Node believes Circle suppressed their insights → Circle says: "We hear that. We are sorry our actions felt that way. And, we acted to keep you safe. Both can be true. We are open to discussing this when emotions have settled."

4. **Repair processes are available, not mandatory.** If the node's interpretation has caused harm (e.g., false accusations), restorative processes are offered. But the node cannot be forced to apologize or recant their interpretation.

5. **Time and presence over debate.** Arguing about the "truth" of a psychotic experience is futile and often harmful. The Circle's role is to maintain safety, offer consistent presence, and allow time for perspectives to shift organically.

**Long-term reality:**
Some nodes will never "agree" with the Circle's framing of their crisis. They will always believe they were not ill, but seeing clearly. This does not revoke their belonging. The Circle learns to coexist with this difference, provided behavior remains within community norms.

**When interpretations persist and cause ongoing harm:**

If after 3 months post-crisis, a node's interpretation still involves:
- persistent accusations against specific individuals
- refusal to coexist peacefully with those accused
- ongoing recruitment of others into the narrative

Then structured intervention is required:
- mediation with neutral third party (defined in §3D)
- temporary physical distance if needed
- clear behavioral contract
- possible transition to different Circle if coexistence impossible

The node's belonging to Flow is not revoked. Their participation in *this specific Circle* may need to change.

---

### §3A.3 BEHAVIORAL RED LINES

**Principle:** Belief is free. Behavior has limits.

The following behaviors **always** require immediate intervention, regardless of the node's interpretive framework:

**Physical safety threats:**
- Threats of violence, even vague or symbolic
- Possession or acquisition of weapons
- Stalking or following behavior
- Attempts to physically harm self or others
- Blocking exits or physically trapping people

**Relational boundaries:**
- Repeated, targeted accusations against specific individuals (more than 3 times in 24 hours)
- Harassment via any medium (in-person, digital, written)
- Intrusion into others' private spaces (homes, rooms, devices)
- Creating climate of fear or intimidation
- Sustained screaming at close range intended to intimidate

**Collective sustainability:**
- Sabotage of shared resources (food, water, infrastructure)
- Deliberate disruption of others' Baseline (sleep deprivation, noise, blocking access)
- Financial manipulation or theft
- Destruction of common property

**Consent violations:**
- Any form of sexual boundary crossing
- Coercion or manipulation
- Ignoring explicitly stated "no"

**These red lines apply to everyone, regardless of mental state.**

Crossing a red line triggers:
1. Immediate intervention
2. Separation if needed for safety (see §8.1A)
3. Documentation
4. Professional consultation
5. Behavioral contract before return to full participation

Mental illness explains behavior. It does not excuse harm.

## §3B. DECISION AUTHORITY IN CRISIS STATES

**Problem:**  
Protocols describe what should happen, but not who decides when it happens.  
This creates confusion, delays, and conflicts during actual crises.

**Solution: The Crisis Lead role**

### Structure:

**1. Every Circle designates a Crisis Lead**
- Rotated role, 1-3 month terms
- Not a permanent authority, but a temporary decision-maker
- Can be declined by anyone who feels unable to bear the weight
- **Role Protection:** No one may be Crisis Lead more than 3 times per year unless they explicitly volunteer for additional terms

**2. Crisis Lead mandate:**
The Crisis Lead has authority to:
- Initiate crisis protocols
- Activate Ulysses Contracts
- Decide on temporary separation
- Contact professional services
- Make safety-critical decisions in real-time

**3. Safeguards:**
- All Crisis Lead decisions are **documented immediately** (within 1 hour)
- All decisions are **reviewed by the full Circle within 72 hours**
- Decisions are **audited by an external Observer** (see §3E) for major interventions (involuntary hospitalization, permanent separation)
- Crisis Lead can be overridden by unanimous Circle consensus
- Crisis Lead must step down if personally involved in the crisis (romantic partner, active conflict, family member)

**4. When Crisis Lead is unavailable:**
- Secondary Crisis Lead must be designated
- If both unavailable: any two Circle members can jointly activate protocol
- These joint decisions receive same 72-hour review

**5. Limits of Crisis Lead authority:**
Crisis Lead **cannot**:
- Make permanent decisions about the node's membership
- Modify Baseline guarantees
- Act in contradiction to documented Ulysses Contracts without Circle review
- Use authority for personal grievances

**6. Cooling-off and external review:**
After any major crisis intervention, a **mandatory 7-day cooling-off period** occurs before final decisions:
- External Observer reviews all documentation
- Node (if able) provides their perspective
- Circle discusses with external input
- Adjustments made if needed

**Principle:**  
Clear authority during chaos saves lives.  
Distributed review after crisis preserves collective wisdom.

Both are necessary.

## §3C. POWER DYNAMICS AND SAFEGUARDS

**Reality check:**  
Circles are not neutral, harmonious collectives. Power imbalances always exist:
- age and life experience
- neurotype (neurotypical vs neurodivergent)
- social capital and charisma
- language fluency
- economic resources
- physical ability
- cultural/racial privilege
- gender dynamics

**These imbalances shape who gets believed, whose boundaries are respected, and whose "crisis" is taken seriously.**

### Mandatory safeguards:

**1. No unilateral decisions**
Major interventions (separation, professional escalation, Ulysses Contract activation) require **minimum two people** to agree.

Exception: immediate physical danger (violence, suicide attempt) allows single-person action, but must be reported to Circle within 1 hour.

**2. Conflict of interest rules**
The following people **may not** be sole decision-makers in a crisis:
- Current or former romantic/sexual partners of the node
- Blood relatives
- People with active conflicts with the node
- People mentioned in the node's Ulysses Contract as potentially unsafe
- People who have had serious conflicts with the node in past 6 months

**3. Documentation of disagreement**
Circles must record **dissent**, not just consensus.

If intervention happens over objections:
- objections are documented with full reasoning
- reasons for proceeding anyway are documented
- automatic external review is triggered
- dissenting members' perspectives are included in post-crisis evaluation

**4. External consultation requirement**
For major decisions (involuntary hospitalization, permanent separation, ending care), Circle must:
- consult at least one external Observer (see §3E)
- document this consultation
- seriously consider their input
- document why input was followed or not followed

**5. Annual power audit**
Once per year, Circle conducts explicit review facilitated by external party:
- Who makes most decisions?
- Whose voice is heard first?
- Whose concerns are minimized?
- Are there patterns related to gender, race, neurotype, etc.?
- What needs to change?

This is uncomfortable. It is also necessary.

**Audit questions:**
- Did the same person act as Crisis Lead more than others? Why?
- Were decisions about women/POC/disabled nodes different from decisions about others?
- Did anyone burn out due to implicit expectations based on gender/caring roles?

**Principle:**  
Power cannot be eliminated.  
It can be made visible, rotated, and accountable.

---

## §3D. DEFINITION OF "NEUTRAL THIRD PARTY"

When mediation or external consultation is required, a Neutral Third Party must meet these criteria:

**Required characteristics:**
- No personal relationship with any involved parties
- No financial interest in outcome
- Trained in conflict resolution, mediation, or mental health support
- Can be trusted to maintain confidentiality

**Possible sources:**
- Member of another Flow Circle (minimum 50km away from this Circle)
- Professional mediator (paid if needed from Circle resources)
- Trusted community elder with mediation experience
- Mental health professional with trauma-informed training
- Peer support specialist with lived experience of mental health crisis

**What they do:**
- Listen to all perspectives without judgment
- Help identify core needs and conflicts
- Facilitate conversations that have become stuck
- Provide reality checks when dynamics are unhealthy
- Support repair processes

**What they do NOT do:**
- Make decisions for the Circle
- Force resolution
- Take sides
- Share private information without consent

**Compensation:**
If Neutral Third Party is a professional, they should be compensated fairly from Circle resources or Flow network funds.

---

## §3E. THE OBSERVER - EXTERNAL AUDIT FUNCTION

**Purpose:** To prevent power abuse and ensure protocol compliance during high-stakes decisions.

**When Observer is activated:**
- Involuntary hospitalization
- Permanent separation from Circle
- Violence has occurred
- Ulysses Contract being used to override node's current wishes
- Any decision that permanently affects node's life

**Who can be an Observer:**
- Member of Flow network from different region
- Mental health professional with values alignment
- Trusted person from node's Ulysses Contract (if specified)
- Legal advocate if legal issues involved

**Observer's role:**
- Review all documentation within 48 hours
- Interview key parties (with consent)
- Assess if protocol was followed
- Check for power abuse or bias
- Provide written assessment to Circle
- Recommend adjustments if needed

**Observer's authority:**
- Can flag violations of protocol
- Can recommend reversal of decisions
- Cannot unilaterally override Circle (unless legal violations found)
- Report goes to both Circle and node

**Compensation:**
Observers are paid for their time unless they volunteer.

**Principle:**  
External eyes protect both the node and the Circle from invisible dynamics.

## §4. CONDITION-SPECIFIC GUIDANCE

### Bipolar Disorder – Type I

**Manic indicators may include:**
- grandiosity and inflated certainty
- impulsive risk-taking
- reduced sleep with increased activity
- erosion of consent and financial judgment

**Required actions:**
- Contact designated emergency contact
- Reduce stimulation and external demands
- Temporarily protect shared resources and finances
- Prioritize safety over autonomy during acute mania

**Depressive indicators may include:**
- withdrawal and isolation
- hopelessness or self-erasure
- suicidal ideation

**Required actions:**
- Daily check-ins
- Remove access to means of self-harm where possible
- Reduce expectations and responsibilities to zero during acute phases
- Maintain calm presence without pressure or correction

*Note:* Zero expectations are a **temporary protective state**, not a permanent identity.

**Post-episode care:**
After a manic or depressive episode, node may experience:
- Shame about behavior during mania
- Grief over time/money/relationships lost
- Fear of it happening again

**Support includes:**
- Explicit reassurance that they are not their episode
- Help repairing relationships damaged during crisis
- Space to process without pressure to "move on"
- Adjustment of responsibilities gradually, not suddenly

---

### Schizophrenia & Psychosis

**Guiding rule:**  
Validate emotional experience. Do not validate delusional content **during acute crisis**.

**Distinction:**  
There is a difference between:
- **Active crisis management:** "I hear that you're terrified. Let's get you safe." (validating emotion, not arguing about content)
- **Post-crisis exploration:** "Tell me what you experienced. What did it mean to you?" (allowing the node to construct their own narrative)

**Actions during crisis:**
- Maintain calm, grounded communication
- Avoid arguing about reality or "convincing"
- Contact psychiatric or crisis services promptly
- Support continuity of prescribed medication

**Actions after stabilization:**
- Create space for the node to process the experience without judgment
- Respect their right to find meaning, pattern, or insight in what occurred
- Support them in distinguishing between what was signal and what was noise — but let them make that determination

**Example:**  
A node reports hearing "the trees communicating underground" during a psychotic episode. During crisis: ensure safety. After crisis: they may discover this matches emerging research on mycelial networks. Or they may conclude it was pure hallucination. Either way — their interpretation, not ours.

---

### Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)

**Core tension:**  
Intense emotional pain combined with fear of abandonment.

**Required stance:**  
Boundaries *and* compassion — simultaneously.

**Actions:**
- Use clear, consistent limits
- Avoid sudden withdrawal or punitive distancing
- Validate pain without absorbing responsibility
- Explicitly refuse the therapist role
- Never make empty promises or threats
- Follow through on stated boundaries every time

Consistency is care.  
Inconsistency escalates harm.

**What this looks like:**
- "I care about you AND I cannot respond to messages after 10pm"
- "Your pain is real AND I cannot fix it for you"
- "I will be here tomorrow at 3pm like I said" (and then be there)

## §4A. VIOLENCE AND THREAT PROTOCOL

**Reality:** Mental illness sometimes includes violence. Pretending otherwise endangers everyone.

### What counts as violence:

**Physical:**
- Hitting, pushing, restraining
- Throwing objects at people
- Blocking exits or trapping people
- Intentional property destruction meant to intimidate
- Spitting, biting, scratching

**Verbal:**
- Explicit threats ("I will hurt you")
- Implicit threats ("Something bad might happen to you")
- Sustained screaming at close range meant to intimidate
- Slurs, dehumanizing language meant to degrade
- Rape threats or sexual violence threats

**Symbolic:**
- Displaying weapons
- Leaving threatening notes or images
- Simulating violence (choking gestures, mock hits)
- Deliberate destruction of cherished belongings

### Immediate actions when violence occurs:

**1. Protect people first**
- Remove others from the space immediately
- Do not attempt physical restraint unless trained
- Create physical distance
- Call for backup from other Circle members

**2. Call for help**
- Crisis Lead or any two Circle members can call emergency services
- In Sweden: 112 for police/ambulance, or psychiatric emergency at local hospital
- Document exactly what happened (time, location, witnesses, injuries)
- Do NOT minimize or explain away
- Photo documentation of injuries/damage if possible

**3. Activate separation immediately**
- Node is moved to separate space or asked to leave
- This is not punishment; this is safety
- See §8.1A for how to separate safely

**4. Professional consultation required**
- Circle cannot handle violence alone
- Psychiatric or crisis services must be involved
- Document all professional recommendations
- Follow professional recommendations unless they violate Baseline

### After violence:

**1. Care for those harmed**
- Medical attention for any injuries
- Emotional support and processing
- Space to feel scared/angry without minimizing
- Right to set boundaries about future contact

**2. Mandatory review process**
- External Observer activated automatically
- What led to this?
- What warning signs were missed?
- What needs to change?
- Full Circle discussion within 7 days

**3. Conditions for return**
- Professional psychiatric assessment
- Medication adjustment if relevant
- Explicit behavioral contract with consequences
- Gradual reintegration (day visits → overnight → full participation)
- Unanimous Circle consent required

**4. Zero-tolerance boundary**
If violence recurs after one return:
- Permanent separation from this specific Circle
- Transition to professional care setting
- Baseline maintained, belonging to Flow honored
- But this Circle cannot be the care structure

**Principle:**  
Mental illness can coexist with accountability.  
The node is not evil. The behavior is still unacceptable.  
Compassion for the person. Zero tolerance for violence.

## §4B. WHEN A NODE REFUSES ALL FORMS OF CARE

**Scenario:**  
Node is in severe crisis (psychotic, manic, severely depressed, actively suicidal) AND refuses:
- medication
- professional help
- Circle support
- separation
- any intervention

**This is one of the hardest situations a Circle will face.**

### Circle's response framework:

**1. Minimum safety floor**
Circle defines non-negotiable baseline:
- Access to food and water
- Shelter
- Someone checks in daily (even if node refuses to engage)

**If node refuses even this, Circle has reached its limit.**

**Escalation threshold:**
If the node cannot reliably meet food, sleep, or physical safety needs for 48 hours, escalation to professional/state systems is required regardless of refusal.

**2. Document refusal clearly**
- What was offered
- What was refused
- When and by whom
- Node's stated reasons (if given)
- Witness signatures

This documentation protects both node and Circle legally and ethically.

**3. Escalate to professional/legal systems if:**
- Immediate danger to life (suicide attempt, severe self-harm)
- Immediate danger to others
- Complete inability to meet own Baseline (no food for 48hrs, exposure to elements)
- Medical emergency (seizures, overdose, severe dehydration)

**In these cases, Circle may need to:**
- Contact family (if appropriate and listed in Ulysses Contract)
- Call crisis services (Sweden: psychiatric emergency assessment, 112)
- In extreme cases: support psychiatric emergency services in involuntary assessment (LPT process in Sweden)

This feels like betrayal. It is sometimes necessary.

**4. Acknowledge the limit**
Circle tells the node clearly:
> "We want to support you. You have refused all forms of help we can offer. We cannot keep you safe if you will not allow any intervention. We are going to [specific action: call psychiatric emergency/ambulance/family]. We do this with grief, not anger. Your Baseline and belonging remain. But we cannot do nothing while you are in danger."

**5. Maintain Baseline throughout**
Even if node is in professional care against their will:
- Circle visits if allowed and if node wants visits
- Circle ensures basic needs are met in facility
- Circle advocates for humane treatment
- Circle does not abandon

**6. Post-crisis reconciliation**
When node stabilizes:
- Circle acknowledges the violence of forced intervention
- Circle explains why it felt necessary
- Circle listens to node's experience of what happened
- Circle and node work to repair trust
- Circle learns from what happened

**Principle:**  
Autonomy is sacred.  
Life is more sacred.  

When these collide, we choose life — then we grieve the violation, repair what we can, and learn.

## §5. THE ULYSSES CONTRACT

Nodes may, during stable periods, create a **Ulysses Contract**.

This is a pre-committed instruction stating:
> "If I enter a crisis state, my previously expressed sovereign wishes override my current impaired judgment."

### Structure and safeguards:

**Ulysses Contracts must include:**
- emergency contacts
- permitted interventions
- acceptable autonomy restrictions
- non-negotiable safety boundaries
- people who should NOT be involved in care decisions
- preferences for professional intervention
- **Digital access provisions:** who can access/lock which accounts, devices, or financial assets during crisis

**Legal safeguards:**
- Must be witnessed by minimum 2 neutral people (not romantic partners or family)
- Must include date created
- Is valid for maximum 12 months, then must be renewed
- Can be revised at any time while node is in stable state
- Includes "Dead Man's Switch" for digital assets: specific person gains temporary access to lock accounts/credit cards to prevent financial ruin during mania

**Review for Baseline compliance:**
Before accepting an Ulysses Contract, Circle reviews to ensure it does NOT:
- Violate Baseline guarantees (housing, food, dignity)
- Contain elements of self-punishment or shame
- Give authority to people with conflicts of interest
- Include interventions the Circle cannot realistically provide

If concerns exist, Circle works with node to revise.

**Use in crisis:**
- Crisis Lead can activate Ulysses Contract
- Activation must be documented and reviewed within 72 hours
- If node strongly objects to contract terms during crisis, Circle must balance contract against current distress
- External Observer is activated for any major use of Ulysses Contract

**Template provided in Appendix C.**

**Safeguard:**  
Ulysses Contracts guide action — they do not replace judgment, care, or contextual awareness.

They are instruments of sovereignty, not tools of control.

## §6. CAREGIVER PROTECTION & BURNOUT PREVENTION

Supporting severe mental illness is inherently taxing.

Flow therefore mandates structured limits:

### Operational caregiver limits:

**Maximum hours per week:**
- Primary caregiver: 20 hours maximum
- Secondary caregiver: 15 hours maximum
- Anyone else: 10 hours maximum

**Maximum consecutive days:**
- No one carries primary responsibility more than 3 consecutive days
- Mandatory 2-day break after 3-day shift

**Mandatory handover protocol:**
- Written log of what happened during shift (template in Appendix)
- Verbal briefing to next caregiver (minimum 15 minutes)
- Emotional check-in for outgoing caregiver
- Incoming caregiver confirms they are in good-enough state to take over

**Access to Refugium:**
- Caregivers have guaranteed access to Refugium or equivalent rest space
- No questions asked, no guilt, no justification needed
- Minimum 4 hours per week during active care periods
- Can be emergency accessed mid-shift if needed

**Circle check-ins:**
- Weekly caregiver circle to share load, vent, process
- External facilitator if possible (someone not in caregiving role)
- Safe space to express difficult feelings (anger, resentment, fear, exhaustion)

**No node may destroy their own Baseline in the act of caring for another.**

Burnout is a system failure, not a personal one.

If caregivers are consistently exceeding limits, the system must change:
- Expand the Circle
- Reduce the node's expectations
- Transition care to professional settings
- Acknowledge capacity limits (see §8.1)

**Soft abandonment is still abandonment.**  
It just happens slowly and guiltily instead of cleanly and honestly.

## §7. FAILURE MODES TO AVOID

The following constitute system failure:
- shaming or moralizing illness
- abandonment disguised as "respecting autonomy"
- coercion disguised as care
- caregivers silently exceeding their limits
- unacknowledged power imbalances
- violence minimized or explained away
- believing that love alone is enough
- making decisions based on who is loudest or most charismatic
- ignoring Gender/race/class dynamics in who becomes caregiver

Care without boundaries harms.  
Boundaries without care abandon.

Flow requires both.

## §8. BELONGING VS. PARTICIPATION

**Critical distinction:**

**Belonging is unconditional.**  
Every node belongs to Flow regardless of:
- mental state
- capacity
- behavior
- crisis history
- recovery trajectory

**Participation is conditional on behavior and safety.**  
Participation in a specific Circle requires:
- respecting behavioral red lines
- allowing others to maintain their Baseline
- working within the structure even when disagreeing with it

**What this means:**
- A node separated from a Circle due to violence still belongs to Flow
- A node who never "agrees" they were ill still belongs to Flow
- A node in permanent professional care still belongs to Flow
- A node who needs to move to a different Circle still belongs to Flow

**Belonging means:**
- Right to interpretation of experience
- Right to Baseline
- Right to dignity
- Connection to Flow network
- Welcome at gatherings and events
- Access to Refugium

**Participation means:**
- Active role in a specific Circle
- Shared decision-making
- Caregiving and receiving care
- Living in shared physical space

These can be separated.

## §8.1 WHEN A CIRCLE REACHES CAPACITY LIMITS

**Reality:**  
Sometimes a Circle cannot safely or sustainably support a node's level of need.

This is not moral failure. This is structural reality.

**Indicators that capacity is exceeded:**

**If TWO OR MORE persist for 14 days despite intervention:**
- Caregivers consistently burning out despite rotation
- Other nodes' Baseline being compromised to maintain one node's care
- Violence or threats becoming weekly or more frequent
- Professional services refusing or unable to help
- Node's condition worsening despite all efforts
- Circle members developing secondary trauma
- Essential Circle functions (work, childcare, cooking) failing

**When this happens, the Circle must:**

**1. Name it explicitly**
In a Circle meeting, with the node present if possible:
> "We have reached our capacity limit. We cannot safely continue this level of care without harm to ourselves and others."

**2. No blame**
- This is not the node's fault for being "too sick"
- This is not the Circle's fault for being "not good enough"
- This is system-level mismatch between need and capacity

**3. Maintain Baseline throughout transition**
- Housing continues (or equivalent arranged)
- Food continues
- Dignity continues
- Someone still checks in
- This is said with grief, not anger

**4. Active transition support:**
- Help the node find alternative living/care arrangements (family, supported housing, professional facility, other Node with more capacity)
- Maintain Baseline throughout transition
- Offer continued relationship (visits, calls, ongoing connection) if the node desires it
- Support advocacy if moving into institutional care

**5. Acknowledge the loss:**
- The Circle grieves this. It is a form of community limitation, even when unavoidable
- Document lessons learned to improve future capacity
- Share learnings with Flow network

**What this is NOT:**
- Exile or abandonment
- "You're too sick for us"
- Washing hands of responsibility

**What this IS:**
- Honoring limits
- Prioritizing the node's actual wellbeing over the Circle's desire to feel virtuous
- A painful, necessary act of love

**Principle:** It is better to honestly transition care than to silently resent and eventually explode.

## §8.1A. PSYCHOLOGICAL SAFETY DURING SEPARATION

**Problem:**  
For people with trauma, attachment disorders, or BPD, separation can feel like death.  
If separation is done carelessly, it causes severe retraumatization.

### How to separate without abandonment:

**Before separation:**
- Calm, clear explanation (not during peak crisis if avoidable)
- Validation of feelings: "I know this feels terrible. It is not punishment. We are doing this to keep everyone safe, including you."
- Specific timeframe: "This separation will last [X hours/days]. Then we reassess."
- Name who will check in and when: "Every day at 10am, [Name] will visit for 15 minutes."
- Explain what's available during separation: "You have phone access. You have food. The door is not locked."

**Trauma-informed communication during separation announcement:**
- Use "I" statements: "I am worried about your safety" not "You are dangerous"
- Acknowledge their reality: "I hear that you feel we're trying to control you. That's not our intention. Our intention is safety."
- Offer choice where possible: "Would you prefer to stay in the downstairs room or at [backup location]?"
- Never threaten: "If you don't calm down we'll have to separate you" → Instead: "I'm concerned about safety. I need to make sure everyone, including you, is okay. That might mean we need some space."

**During separation:**
- Predictable, scheduled check-ins (not random)
- Same person checks in each time if possible
- Check-ins are brief (10-15 min), calm, consistent
- Node knows exactly when next contact will be: "Tomorrow at 10am, I'll be back."
- If node is very distressed: "I'm here. I'm not leaving you. You're safe. I'm going to sit here with you for a few minutes."

**What check-ins include:**
- "How are you doing?"
- Confirm basic needs met (food, water, medication)
- Brief calm presence
- "I'll be back [specific time]."

**What check-ins do NOT include:**
- Lectures about behavior
- Demands for apologies
- Rehashing the crisis
- Making promises about when separation will end (unless you can keep them)

**After separation:**
- Structured return conversation (template in Appendix)
- "What did you experience during this?"
- "What do you need from us going forward?"
- Clear behavioral expectations for return
- Explicit repair if needed: "I'm sorry this was necessary. I know it hurt."
- No punishment, no grudges, no "you have to earn your way back"

**If node refuses all contact during separation:**
- Daily brief message left where they can find it: "We're here. We care. We'll check again tomorrow."
- Leave food/water accessible
- Respect the boundary while maintaining presence
- Don't force entry unless medical emergency

**Special consideration for children witnessing separation:**
If node has children who witness separation:
- Age-appropriate explanation: "Parent needs some quiet time to feel better. They're safe. You're safe."
- Maintain child's routine as much as possible
- Check in with child's emotional state
- Protect child from details of crisis

**Principle:**  
Separation is not exile.  
It is temporary distance for safety, held with care.

## §8.2 AFTERCARE AND REINTEGRATION

**After any major crisis (hospitalization, separation, violence, forced intervention), the node needs structured support to return.**

### First Week Back - Reintegration Protocol:

**Day 1-3:**
- No requirements to participate in meetings or work
- Node can be silent, avoid eye contact, stay in their space
- One designated "bridge person" checks in daily (someone who was NOT involved in crisis decisions if possible)
- Bridge person says: "I'm glad you're back. No pressure. I'm here if you need anything."

**Day 4-7:**
- Gentle invitation (not requirement) to join meals or activities
- Still no expectations of productivity
- Bridge person continues check-ins
- Node can request time to talk about what happened (but it's not required)

**Week 2:**
- Gradual return to shared responsibilities
- Explicit conversation: "What feels manageable? What feels too much?"
- Adjust based on node's capacity, not Circle's preferences

**Post-Crisis Meaning-Making (optional, node-initiated):**
When node is ready (could be weeks or months later):
- Facilitated conversation about their experience
- Node writes or speaks their narrative of what happened
- Narrative is recorded in Circle memory alongside crisis documentation as alternative perspective
- Both versions exist simultaneously - no need to agree on "truth"

**Post-Crisis Debrief (mandatory, Circle-initiated, within 14 days):**

Using template in Appendix D, Circle reviews:
- What worked?
- What felt like overreach?
- What did we learn about this node's specific patterns?
- What do we need to do differently next time?
- How are caregivers doing?
- What repairs are needed?

This becomes institutional knowledge for this specific node.

**Principle:**  
Coming back should not require performance of "being better."  
It requires safety and willingness to coexist.

## §9. FINAL PRINCIPLE

Severe mental illness changes capacity — not worth.

A node in crisis remains a node of the system.  
They are not exiled.  
They are not reduced.  
They are not erased.

Care continues.  
Dignity remains.  
Belonging is not revoked.

Some nodes will emerge from severe mental illness with scars.  
Some will emerge with scars **and** knowledge.

The system does not decide which.  
The node does.

Flow's role is to keep them alive and safe while they figure it out.

**And:**  
No node should ever have to apologize for becoming ill.  
But all nodes have responsibility to not make others ill with their suffering when they can influence it.

## §10. LOCAL ADAPTATION NOTES

Severe mental illness protocols must respect both **universal principles** and **local realities**. The following guidance ensures Circles remain effective and compliant in diverse contexts:

### 10.1 Legal and Healthcare Context
- Identify local mental health laws, patient rights, and involuntary treatment regulations  
- In Sweden: Learn LPT (psychiatric involuntary care), LVU (youth care), patient rights law, GDPR for documentation
- Map available psychiatric services, crisis hotlines (Sweden: 112, local psychiatric emergency, 1177), emergency care facilities  
- Ensure all interventions comply with national and regional legislation

### 10.2 Cultural Considerations
- Respect local attitudes toward mental illness, caregiving norms, and family involvement  
- Adapt communication styles and consent processes to align with cultural expectations without compromising dignity or Baseline guarantees
- Examples:
  - Collectivist cultures: May prefer family involvement in decisions (adjust privacy boundaries)
  - Indigenous communities: May integrate traditional healing practices (document and respect)
  - Religious communities: May want spiritual leaders involved (with node consent)

### 10.3 Resource Availability
- Adjust support protocols based on local access to medication, therapy, and care infrastructure  
- In low-resource settings, prioritize:
  - Stabilization of immediate environment  
  - Refugium or temporary safe spaces  
  - Remote professional consultation where possible (telehealth, phone consultations)
  - Peer support networks
  - Traditional healers if culturally appropriate and node consents

### 10.4 Crisis Escalation
- Define region-specific pathways for urgent intervention, including emergency contacts, transportation, and professional support  
- Sweden-specific: psychiatric emergency assessment (akutmottagning), SIP (Structured Individual Plan) for chronic conditions, care coordinator (vårdsamordnare)
- Document local procedures clearly for all Circle members to prevent confusion during acute episodes

### 10.5 Documentation and Feedback
- Record all adaptations made for local conditions in the Circle log  
- Share learnings with the broader Flow network to improve global protocols
- Protect documentation legally: store encrypted, limit access, comply with GDPR/local privacy laws

> **Principle:** Adaptation is contextual; the **core commitment to Baseline, dignity, and belonging** is universal.

## §11. WHEN PROFESSIONAL CARE IS UNAVAILABLE OR UNSAFE

**Reality:** In many contexts, "appropriate professional systems" are:
- Non-existent (rural areas, underfunded regions)
- Inaccessible (cost, distance, waiting lists - common even in Sweden for non-acute care)
- Actively harmful (abusive institutions, forced medication without consent, cultural incompetence, racist treatment)

**When this occurs, the Circle's role shifts from "routing" to "harm reduction with presence."**

### Minimum viable care when external help is absent:

**1. Safety-first containment:**
- Create low-stimulation space (quiet, dim, predictable)
- Remove access to immediate dangers (weapons, heights, vehicles, medications that could be overdosed)
- Ensure at least one calm person present at all times (rotated every 4-6 hours maximum)

**2. Non-professional de-escalation:**
- Speak slowly, softly, with simple sentences
- Validate emotion without validating dangerous content
- "I hear you're terrified. You're safe right now. I'm here."
- Never argue about reality. Never lie. Never make promises you can't keep.
- Offer simple choices: "Water or tea?" "Sit or lie down?" "Light on or off?"

**3. Documentation for future advocacy:**
- Record what happened, when, what was tried
- Creates evidence if/when professional care becomes accessible
- Protects both node and Circle from future accusations
- Can be used to advocate for better services

**4. Clear limits:**
- Circle CAN: provide calm presence, ensure Baseline, prevent immediate harm, call emergency services
- Circle CANNOT: diagnose, prescribe, medicate, provide therapy, guarantee safety beyond basic containment
- If situation exceeds these limits, Circle must acknowledge this openly

**Acknowledgment of failure:**
Sometimes, despite best efforts, a Circle cannot keep someone safe without professional help.

This is not moral failure. This is reality.

When this occurs:
- Document limits honestly
- Seek any available external support (telehealth, neighboring Nodes, crisis hotlines, emergency services)
- If all fails: prioritize protecting the collective while maintaining the node's Baseline

## §11A. RELATIONSHIP TO STATE PSYCHIATRIC SYSTEMS

**Tension:**  
Flow respects professional expertise AND knows that state systems can be coercive, racist, ableist, and harmful.

**Flow's position:**

**1. Professional care is valuable**
- Medication saves lives
- Therapy supports healing
- Crisis intervention prevents death
- Diagnosis helps some people understand themselves

**2. State systems can also harm**
- Involuntary commitment is traumatic
- Restraints cause injury and retraumatization
- Medications have severe side effects
- Marginalized people (POC, immigrants, LGBTQ, neurodivergent) face disproportionate coercion
- Cultural incompetence causes additional harm

**3. Flow works WITH systems where possible, AROUND them when necessary**

### Decision Protocol for State System Engagement:

**Ask in this order:**

1. **Is death or severe irreversible harm imminent?** (Yes = engage state system immediately)
2. **Does node explicitly consent to professional care?** (Yes = engage system with their preferences)
3. **Does Ulysses Contract specify this intervention?** (Yes = engage system as specified)
4. **Can Circle safely contain without state involvement?** (No = engage system)
5. **Will state involvement cause more harm than help?**

For question 5, consider:
- Node's demographics (will they face discrimination?)
- Node's past trauma with psychiatric system
- Quality of local psychiatric services
- Availability of culturally competent care
- Language barriers
- Immigration status concerns

**Document reasoning for decision in all cases.**

### When engaging state systems:

**Before:**
- Explain to node what will happen
- If possible, let them choose which hospital/service
- Bring their medication list, Ulysses Contract if exists
- Designate Circle member to accompany if possible

**During:**
- Circle maintains contact if node allows
- Circle advocates for humane treatment
- Circle brings comfort items if allowed
- Circle monitors for abuse/neglect

**After:**
- Circle acknowledges the violation of forced intervention
- Circle works to repair trust
- Circle supports node in processing the experience
- Circle learns from what happened

### Alternative care pathways:

When state systems are unsafe or unavailable:
- Peer support networks (other Flow Nodes, mental health peer specialists)
- Telehealth services (online therapy, psychiatric consultation)
- Traditional healers (if culturally appropriate and node consents)
- Community mental health workers
- Faith-based support (if node consents)
- Harm reduction approach with Circle support

**Document alternative pathway chosen and why.**

**Principle:**  
We do not romanticize mental illness or demonize treatment.  
We also do not pretend state systems are neutral or always safe.  

We make hard choices in impossible situations, document them, and live with the consequences together.

## §11B. LOW-CAPACITY MODE

**Reality:**  
Not all Circles have the resources, skills, or energy for full protocol implementation.

When Circle is small, new, exhausted, or under-resourced:

### Activation of Low-Capacity Mode:

**Triggered when ANY of these are true:**
- Circle has fewer than 5 active members
- More than half of caregivers are burned out
- Crisis has lasted more than 30 days
- Professional help completely unavailable
- Multiple crises happening simultaneously

### Low-Capacity Mode priorities:

**CORE FUNCTIONS ONLY:**
1. **Safety** (prevent immediate death/severe harm)
2. **Food** (ensure node eats something daily)
3. **Sleep** (protect node's sleep and others' sleep)
4. **Presence** (someone checks in daily for minimum 10 minutes)

**Everything else is paused:**
- Behavioral contracts
- Restorative justice processes
- Deep processing conversations
- Ulysses Contract creation
- Circle meetings beyond crisis essentials
- Non-emergency projects

**Document limitations openly:**

Tell the node:
> "We are in Low-Capacity Mode. We can provide: safety checks, food, presence, and emergency help. We cannot provide: intensive support, long conversations, or complex care. This is temporary."

Tell the Circle:
> "We are in Low-Capacity Mode. Survival priorities only. No guilt for what we can't do."

Tell external advisors/Flow network:
> "We need backup. Our capacity is exceeded."

**Prioritize external help:**
- Call on other Nodes for support
- Use professional services even if imperfect
- Activate family if safe
- Use crisis hotlines
- Request emergency Flow network resources

**Shorter, more frequent rotations:**
- Instead of 8-hour shifts: 2-4 hour shifts
- Instead of 3-day stretches: 1-day on, 1-day off, or even morning/evening splits
- Reduces burnout, maintains presence

**Lower expectations across the board:**
- For the node in crisis: Baseline only, nothing more
- For caregivers: Show up, be present, go home. That's enough.
- For the collective: We're in crisis mode. Good enough is good enough.

### Exiting Low-Capacity Mode:

**When crisis stabilizes OR Circle gains capacity:**
- Explicit announcement: "We are exiting Low-Capacity Mode as of [date]"
- Gradual restoration of full protocols over 2 weeks
- Review what was learned
- Celebrate survival

**If Low-Capacity Mode lasts more than 60 days:**
- This is a signal that permanent change is needed
- Consider: permanent capacity increase, node transition, or Circle restructure

**Principle:**  
A small amount of care sustained is better than ambitious care that collapses.  
Survival is success.

## §11C. REFUGIUM FOR NODES (NOT JUST CAREGIVERS)

**Clarification:** Refugium is not only for caregivers.

**Nodes in early crisis or prodromal states can also use Refugium:**

**Voluntary Refugium access for nodes:**
- Node notices they're becoming unstable but not yet in crisis
- Node can request "quiet time in Refugium" for hours or days
- This is **preventive**, not reactive
- Does NOT trigger full crisis protocol
- Does NOT require Crisis Lead activation

**What this looks like:**
- Low-stimulation environment
- Minimal social demands
- Presence of calm support person if desired
- Access to calming activities (art supplies, music, nature)
- Option for complete solitude if preferred

**Duration:**
- Can be hours to several days
- Node decides when to return
- If node is in Refugium for >3 days, Circle checks in to assess if crisis protocol needed

**Benefit:**
Some crises can be prevented or reduced in severity if caught early.

## §12. A NOTE ON MADNESS AND PROPHECY

Throughout human history, the line between "madness" and "visionary perception" has been contested, culturally variable, and often weaponized.

Flow takes no position on where that line is.

We commit to:
- **Safety** when states become dangerous
- **Care** without coercion
- **Humility** about what we cannot know
- **Space** for nodes to construct their own meaning

If someone sees what others cannot, and it destroys them — we intervene.  
If someone sees what others cannot, and it teaches them — we witness.

Often it is both simultaneously.

The psychiatric system is designed to eliminate symptoms.  
Flow is designed to preserve the person — including the parts that don't fit diagnostic categories.

We hold both: the need for treatment and the possibility of truth in altered states.

This is not contradiction.  
This is complexity.

## §13. MANDATORY TRAINING AND PREPARATION

**Reality:** This protocol is too complex to use in real-time without preparation.

### Required Circle preparation:

**Annual training (minimum):**
- Crisis simulation exercise with external facilitator
- Each member takes turns being: node in crisis, Crisis Lead, caregiver, witness
- Practice using crisis language guide
- Practice separation protocol
- Practice decision tree navigation
- Debrief what felt hard, what felt helpful

**Quarterly review:**
- Read through protocol sections
- Update local contact lists
- Review any changes to protocol
- Check in on caregiver limits and rotation schedule

**New member onboarding:**
- Must read entire protocol before joining Circle
- Must participate in crisis simulation within first month
- Must shadow experienced Crisis Lead before taking role

**Ongoing skill-building:**
- Training in trauma-informed communication (recommended annually)
- De-escalation techniques (recommended)
- First aid and CPR (required for at least 2 Circle members)
- Mental Health First Aid certification (recommended for Crisis Leads)

### APPENDIX E: CRISIS LANGUAGE GUIDE

**SAY THIS:**

✓ "I hear that you're terrified. You're safe right now."  
✓ "I can't confirm what you're seeing, but I see that you're in pain."  
✓ "I'm worried about your safety."  
✓ "We need to keep everyone safe, including you."  
✓ "I'm going to sit here with you for a few minutes."  
✓ "Tomorrow at 10am, I'll be back."  
✓ "Your feelings are real. Let's keep you safe while you feel them."  
✓ "I care about you AND I need to set a boundary."  
✓ "This is hard for both of us. I'm staying anyway."

**DON'T SAY THIS:**

✗ "That's not real." / "You're imagining things."  
✗ "You need to calm down."  
✗ "You're being difficult."  
✗ "If you don't X, I'll have to Y." (threats)  
✗ "You're scaring me." (makes it about you)  
✗ "Everyone goes through hard times." (minimizing)  
✗ "You were fine yesterday, why is today different?"  
✗ "Just try to think positive."  
✗ "I don't know when you'll be able to come back." (unless true)  

**OFFER SIMPLE CHOICES:**

✓ "Water or tea?"  
✓ "Sit here or over there?"  
✓ "Light on or off?"  
✓ "Do you want me to stay or give you space?"  

**VALIDATE WITHOUT AGREEING:**

✓ "I hear you saying X believes you're dangerous. That must feel awful."  
✓ "You're experiencing something very intense right now."  
✓ "I don't see what you're seeing, but I trust that it feels real to you."  

**WHEN THEY WON'T TALK:**

✓ "I'm going to sit here quietly. You don't have to talk."  
✓ "I'll check on you in 15 minutes."  
✓ "Whenever you're ready."  

**WHEN YOU MUST INTERVENE AGAINST THEIR WILL:**

✓ "I know you don't want this. I'm doing it anyway because I'm worried about your life."  
✓ "This is going to feel like a betrayal. I'm sorry. I'm choosing your life."  
✓ "You can be angry at me later. Right now I need to keep you safe."
