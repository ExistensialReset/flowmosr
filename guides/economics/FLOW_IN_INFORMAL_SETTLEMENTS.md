## WHAT WE LEARNED FROM RESEARCH

We spent days reading studies from Kibera, Mathare, Dharavi, Khayelitsha. Here's what the research says - not our assumptions, but actual data:

### THE VIOLENCE IS STRUCTURAL, NOT EXCEPTIONAL

**Data from Nairobi informal settlements (2018-2024):**
- 38% of ever-married women (age 15-49) have experienced intimate partner violence
- During COVID-19: gender-based violence reports increased **5-fold**
- Women experience violence on the way to **shared toilets** (sanitation-related violence)
- Adolescent girls: food insecurity **increases** sexual violence risk
- Primary cause of violence identified by women: **idle youth** - but also police corruption, lack of protective infrastructure, tribalism

**What this means for organizing:**
Safety isn't a "nice to have." Women organizing face real risk of being beaten at home after attending meetings. Teen moms get kicked out of school. Police arrest 13-year-olds for "loitering."

Any organizing strategy that doesn't address this **will fail women**.

### CLIMATE ISN'T A FUTURE THREAT - IT'S CURRENT CHAOS

**East Africa (2016-2024):**
- Drought since late 2016 (8+ years)
- **5 consecutive failed rainy seasons** (2020-2023)
- 1.4 million displaced by extreme weather (as of June 2023)
- 2.7 million displaced by drought
- Cholera outbreak Nairobi 2023: 11,181 cases, 196 deaths
- Temperatures projected +0.5 to 2°C (but urban heat island effect in slums makes it worse)
- **95% of cropland is rainfed** (totally vulnerable to rain failure)

**What this means for organizing:**
You cannot plan around "average rainfall." There is no average anymore. Seasons shift. Growing calendars from 5 years ago don't work. Every other year, the harvest fails.

Any strategy based on "stable climate" **will fail**.

### TRAUMA ISN'T INDIVIDUAL - IT'S COLLECTIVE & INTERGENERATIONAL

**Mental health data from Nairobi informal settlements:**
- **57% of women score clinical range for PTSD**
- **75% score clinical range for depression**
- 18% of children (age 6-18) have PTSD after 2007 post-election violence
- Rwanda post-genocide: Children of survivors have **3x higher PTSD risk** (16.5% vs 6.2%)

**What traditional healers say:**
In sub-Saharan Africa, trauma is attributed to **spiritual causes**, not just psychological. Western therapy (talk therapy, individual counseling) often doesn't work. What works: **storytelling in community**, **group healing**, **ritual**, **connection to elders**.

**What this means for organizing:**
You cannot build Flow with people so traumatized they cannot trust. Refugium (rest, breakdown space) is not a luxury. It's a **prerequisite**. Without collective healing, circles dissolve.

### CHILDREN ALREADY LEAD - BUT ADULTS DON'T LISTEN

**Youth organizing in Kibera/Mathare:**
- Girls' Parliament mobilized 1,000+ sanitary pad packs (girls identified the need, adults supported)
- Youth-led Green Project Initiative: organic briquettes from waste (solving energy + unemployment)
- Youth peace mediators: reducing conflicts before police involvement
- "Nothing about us without us" - participatory development principle

**What youth say they need:**
- Safe spaces for socializing/recreation (not just productivity)
- Mental health support (stress from violence, poverty, uncertainty)
- **Voice in decisions**, not just "consultation"

**What this means for organizing:**
If children don't have voice, you're not building Flow - you're building another hierarchy. And they will see through it.

## THE HARD TRUTHS WE HAVE TO SAY

### 1. LAND ACCESS IS NOT "OFTEN AVAILABLE"

We wrote that in v1.0. It was wrong.

**Reality:**
- In many areas, land is what people **kill for**
- Land grabbing by corporations, governments, elites is active and ongoing
- Where communal land exists, it's often **low quality** (flood plains, polluted, near dumpsites)
- Urban slums: **zero land access** - only rooftops, balconies, vertical space

**What this means:**
If you don't have land, rural strategies (gardens, chickens, wells) **don't work**. You need urban adaptations: sack gardens, rooftop systems, negotiated access.

### 2. "LABOR ABUNDANT" ROMANTICIZES EXHAUSTION

We wrote "labor is abundant, cash is scarce" as if it's an advantage.

**Reality:**
"Labor abundant" means:
- Digging a well by hand for 2-4 weeks in brutal heat
- Walking 4 hours/day carrying 20kg water (women/girls do this)
- Farming with hoes because you can't afford tools
- Your **body breaks down by age 35**
- This is why people want machines **even when they can't afford them**

**What this means:**
The goal is to **reduce brutal physical labor**, not celebrate it. Short term (Years 1-3): use labor because no choice. Long term: systems that maintain themselves, tools that reduce burden, **rest**.

### 3. TRADITIONAL SYSTEMS OPPRESSED WOMEN & CHILDREN

We wrote "traditional systems are Flow" in v1.0. That was naive.

**Reality:**
Traditional systems got some things **right**:
- Commons (shared land, water, forests)
- Work parties (collective labor)
- Mutual aid (extended family safety nets)

Traditional systems got some things **wrong**:
- Patriarchy (women excluded from councils, land ownership)
- Harmful practices (FGM, child marriage, forced seclusion)
- Hierarchy (elders dominate youth, men dominate women)
- Caste/class exclusion (some groups shut out entirely)

**Colonialism** did terrible things (enclosed commons, imposed chiefs, monetized everything). **But also** sometimes challenged oppressive traditions (like outlawing slavery in some contexts).

**What this means:**
Flow takes the good (commons, mutual aid) and **leaves the bad** (patriarchy, hierarchy). It adds protections (women's voice, child safety, lottery governance). This will create **conflict with elders who benefited from old hierarchies**.

### 4. ROSCA CAN EXCLUDE THE POOREST

We celebrated ROSCAs (rotating savings) in v1.0 as if they're perfect.

**Reality:**
ROSCAs **work** in many contexts. But they can also:
- Exclude the **absolute poorest** (who can't save $2-5/month)
- Be dominated by **men** (women's ROSCAs smaller, less powerful)
- Be captured by **elites** (leaders favor family/tribe)
- **Collapse** when someone steals the pot (it happens)

**What this means:**
ROSCAs are a tool, not a solution. They need protections: **lottery order** (not favoritism), **women-only pools** (men can't veto), **transparent records**, **consequences for theft**.

### 5. COST ESTIMATES WILL BE WRONG FOR YOUR CONTEXT

We gave specific costs in v1.0 ($50-200 for water, etc.). That was **dangerous**.

**Reality:**
Costs vary **±40% minimum**, probably more. Factors:
- Local material prices (bamboo $2-5 in some areas, $20+ in others)
- Tool availability (can you borrow from neighbors or must you buy?)
- Corruption (do you have to pay bribes to officials?)
- Land costs (free communal vs expensive rental)
- Labor costs (if you pay, rates vary hugely)
- Climate (drought = higher water infrastructure costs)

**What this means:**
**Do not use our numbers as a budget.** They're examples from ideal conditions. Your context may cost 2x or 10x more. Or 50% less. You have to **calculate yourself**.

## IF YOU DECIDE TO TRY THIS ANYWAY

We don't recommend using this document as-is. But if you're in a desperate situation and need ideas, here are principles - not steps.

### PRINCIPLE 1: WATER FIRST, ALWAYS

**Why:**
Women/girls in Kibera, Mathare, Dharavi walk 2-6 hours/day for water. That's **unpaid labor** that prevents everything else. No school. No income work. No organizing. No rest.

**Solving water = unlocking time.**

**Urban options (no land):**
- Rainwater harvesting from rooftops (gutters + storage tank)
- Biosand filters (surface water → safe drinking water)
- Collective negotiation with landlords (piped water to building)
- Community water kiosks (shared infrastructure)

**Rural options (land access):**
- Hand-dug protected well (2-4 weeks labor, concrete ring to prevent contamination)
- Spring protection (if natural spring exists)
- Rainwater storage (5,000-10,000 liter capacity)

**ROSCA example (20 families):**
```
$5/month/family × 20 families = $100/month
Month 1: Buy materials for first rainwater tank
Month 2: Buy biosand filter materials
Month 3: Start well digging supplies
Month 4-12: Continue rotation

After 12 months: Entire community has water access
Total cash: $1,200 ($60/family)
Total labor: 10-15 days/family over year
```

**What happens when it fails:**
- Well dries up: Dig deeper OR accept seasonal use OR find another source
- Tank leaks: Repair with local materials (clay, resin) OR accept smaller capacity
- Biosand filter clogs: Clean sand layer (maintenance every 6 months)

**Critical: Women control water projects**
If men control, women still do the labor but don't get rest time. Water projects **must** be led by the people who currently carry water.

### PRINCIPLE 2: WOMEN'S SAFETY IS NON-NEGOTIABLE

**The data is clear:**
Women organizing face **retaliation**. In Nairobi slums:
- Women beaten at home after attending community meetings
- Sanitation-related violence (rape/attack going to shared toilets)
- Economic abuse (husbands withhold money to prevent participation)
- Social stigma ("bad wife" for having opinions)

**What WON'T work:**
- "50% women in meetings" (if they're beaten at home afterward)
- "Women's voice" (if their voice doesn't count)
- "Gender equality" (abstract principles don't stop violence)

**What MIGHT work:**
1. **Women-only sub-circles**
   - Meet separately from men
   - Control own ROSCA pools
   - Make own decisions
   - Men **cannot** veto, cannot attend, cannot demand reports

2. **Feminist for Peace, Rights and Justice Centre model (Kibera)**
   - **Safe house** for women escaping violence
   - Women who've survived GBV lead the organization
   - Economic empowerment (soap making, small business)
   - SMS platform for reporting violence (ActionAid Kenya model)

3. **Explicit protection protocol:**
   - If a man abuses a woman in the circle: **immediate removal**, no second chance
   - Women can call emergency circle meeting (men must leave)
   - Any decision affecting women: women make it, not men "on behalf of"

**This will create conflict.** 
Some men will leave. Some elders will resist. Some families will choose not to join.

**That's okay. Women's safety > men's comfort.**

### PRINCIPLE 3: CLIMATE CHAOS REQUIRES MULTIPLE STRATEGIES

**DO NOT plan for "average" anything.** There is no average rainfall. No predictable seasons.

**What MIGHT work (hedging strategies):**

**Food:**
- **Diversity** - 10+ crop varieties, not 2-3 (if maize fails, sorghum might survive)
- **Timing** - plant same crop 3 times (staggered by 2 weeks) so if one fails, others might work
- **Mixed systems** - perennials + annuals (trees provide food even in drought)
- **Seed saving** - always save 2x what you need (if next season fails, you have backup)
- **Water capture** - every raindrop stored (swales, ponds, tanks)

**Expect failure:**
- 1 in 3 years: harvest fails completely
- 1 in 3 years: harvest reduced by 50%
- 1 in 3 years: harvest okay

**ROSCA emergency food fund:**
10% of all income → emergency grain storage. When harvest fails, circle eats from storage. No one sells land or animals in crisis.

**Urban adaptation:**
- Vertical sack gardens (spinach, kale, amaranth)
- Fast-growing crops (45-60 day varieties, not 90-120 day)
- Perennials on rooftops (moringa grows year-round)
- **Accept dependency on purchased food** for staples (maize, rice) - grow vegetables only

### PRINCIPLE 4: TRAUMA HEALING BEFORE ORGANIZING

**You cannot build Flow with people who cannot trust.**

**Reality from research:**
- 57% of women in Nairobi slums: PTSD
- 75%: depression
- 18% of children: PTSD from post-election violence

**What Western therapy often misses:**
In sub-Saharan African contexts, trauma is understood as **spiritual**, not just psychological. "Talk therapy" doesn't work for everyone. What works:

**Community-based healing:**
- **Storytelling circles** (Narrative Exposure Therapy adapted with local stories)
- **Group sociotherapy** (Rwanda model - hybrid of trauma healing + rebuilding community trust)
- **HROC workshops** (Healing and Rebuilding Our Communities - 3-day intensive)
- **Traditional healers** (more trusted, more accessible than biomedical practitioners)
- **Ritual and ceremony** (marking transitions, honoring grief)

**Refugium Anima in slum context:**
- No private space? **Collective rest space** (one room, rotating use, mattress on floor)
- No time? **Mandatory rest day** (circle agrees: no one works, we rest together)
- No permission to rest? **Women create own rest protocol** (men can't veto)

**Intergenerational trauma:**
Children of survivors have 3x PTSD risk. **Youth need healing spaces too** - not just "activities," but space to talk about fear, anger, grief.

**This is not optional.**
If you skip trauma healing, circles will dissolve over conflict. Tiny disagreements become explosions. Trust never forms.

### PRINCIPLE 5: CHILDREN AND YOUTH LEAD

**They already do.** Adults just don't listen.

**Examples from Kibera/Mathare:**
- Girls' Parliament: identified sanitary pad gap, mobilized 1,000 packs
- Green Project Initiative: youth created organic briquettes from waste
- Youth peace mediators: reduced conflicts **before** police

**What youth need (their words, not ours):**
- Safe spaces for **socializing** (not just productivity)
- Mental health support (they're stressed too)
- **Voice in decisions** that affect them
- Protection from exploitation (child labor happens, we have to address it)

**Age-appropriate participation:**
- **Under 10:** Play, observe, give input when asked (no heavy labor, no decision burden)
- **10-14:** Light tasks (1-2 hours/day max), voice in decisions affecting them, school non-negotiable
- **15-18:** Adult-level work **if they choose**, but education remains priority, full voice in circle

**Youth-led projects:**
Let them decide. Support, don't control. They see solutions adults miss.

**Example:**
If youth say "we need a soccer field," don't respond "that's not productive." Respond: "how can we make that happen?" Play is not frivolous. Rest is not lazy. Joy is infrastructure.

## URBAN FLOW (SLUM CONTEXTS)

Everything we wrote assumed rural/peri-urban with land access. **That was a massive blind spot.**

60% of Nairobi's population lives in informal settlements on **6% of the land**. Most global South urban poor have **zero land access**.

### What's different in urban slums:

**No land** → Vertical growing, rooftop systems, sack gardens, negotiated landlord access

**Rent is the crisis** → Not mortgages. Rent. 80.9% of Kibera residents have rent arrears. Most evictions happen with 24-48 hours notice.

**Density** → You share toilets with 10-50 other households. No privacy. No space for livestock. No room for workshops.

**Informality** → Government doesn't recognize your settlement as "legal." So building "illegal" infrastructure (wells, toilets) risks demolition.

**Violence** → Idle youth + police corruption + informal strongmen = daily threat. Women's organizing risks gendered retaliation.

### Urban adaptations:

**Food:**
- Sack gardens (vertical system: 6-8 sacks stacked, grow kale/spinach)
- Rooftop moringa (if you have roof access)
- Window box herbs (if you have windows)
- **Accept 80-90% purchased food** - grow greens for nutrition, not calories

**Water:**
- Rooftop rainwater (if landlord allows)
- Biosand filters (shared toilet water? No. But river water yes.)
- **Collective negotiation with landlord** (can we install one tap for whole building?)
- Community kiosk (if government allows)

**Energy:**
- Solar home systems (50-100W panel, small battery, LED lights)
- **Shared solar charging station** (20-30 phones, small income stream)
- Biogas **if** you can access livestock manure (rarely possible in dense urban)

**Rent crisis:**
- Housing cooperative (10-20 families pool to rent large building together)
- **Reduces rent per person 50-70%**
- But: requires trust, legal structure, finding landlord willing

**Organizing strategy:**
- **Start tiny** (3-5 families in same building)
- **Focus on rent + water** (biggest burdens)
- **Women's safety paramount** (urban slums have highest GBV rates)
- **Accept hybrid** (most people stay in Mammon jobs, Flow is partial)

**Kibera vertical garden example (2024 data):**
- 85% food insecure
- Sack gardens: grow kale, amaranth, spinach
- Cost: $5-10 per sack garden (recycled sacks, soil, seeds)
- Output: 1-2kg greens/week (supplements purchased food, doesn't replace)
- Labor: 1 hour/week maintenance
- Reduces food costs ~10-15% (not 50%, but something)

## WHEN VIOLENCE IS THE STRUCTURE

We avoided this in v1.0. We can't anymore.

**Reality:**
In many contexts, the local strongman **has guns**. Police **are corrupt**. There is no one to appeal to. **Violence isn't a risk - it's the baseline.**

**What organizing looks like then:**

### 1. INVISIBILITY AS SURVIVAL

- Don't advertise
- Don't draw attention
- Meet in private homes, not public
- No signs, no labels, no "Flow circle" identification
- **Outsiders don't know you exist**

### 2. WOMEN-ONLY CIRCLES (SAFER)

- Less threatening to strongmen (they don't see women organizing as political)
- But: still risks gendered retaliation
- Mitigation: meet during day, in groups, have exit plans

### 3. ECONOMIC BEFORE POLITICAL

- Start with ROSCA (savings, income)
- **Not** governance, **not** land rights, **not** confronting power
- Once economic base stable, **maybe** escalate
- But accept: some things you can't challenge without dying

### 4. RELATIONSHIP WITH STRONGMAN (RISKY)

Some circles negotiate with local power:
- Pay "protection" (extortion, but framed as cooperation)
- Give strongman "role" (advisor, elder, but **not** decision power)
- Let him feel respected while limiting his actual control

**This is morally complicated. We can't tell you if it's right.**

### 5. ACCEPT PARTIAL FLOW

In some contexts, you build:
- Water access (strongman doesn't care)
- Food gardens (strongman doesn't care)
- Women's savings (strongman doesn't notice)

But you **cannot** build:
- Political voice (strongman crushes)
- Land rights (strongman controls land)
- Justice systems (strongman IS the law)

**That's still worth doing.** Partial Flow saves lives.

### 6. EXIT PLAN

If strongman turns on you:
- Dissolve visible structures
- Scatter
- Knowledge stays in people's heads
- Regroup later, elsewhere, quietly

**This is dark. We know.**
But pretending violence doesn't exist gets people killed.

## APPENDIX B: FLOW CORE PRINCIPLES (BRIEF)

For people new to Flow, here's the minimum you need:

**Baseline:** Everyone gets enough to survive (food, water, shelter, healthcare) **without conditions**. Not charity. **Guarantee**.

**Non-coercion:** No one forces participation. You can leave anytime. No guilt. No punishment.

**LOTUS (lottery governance):** Leadership rotates **randomly** (names drawn from hat/bowl). No one holds power long enough to become corrupt.

**Refugium Anima:** Rest is **infrastructure**, not luxury. Breakdown space. Permission to be broken.

**L × S × I:** Lugn (Calm) × Spontanitet (Spontaneity) × Inkännande (Empathy) = prerequisite nervous system states for Flow

**Circle governance:** 5-30 people make decisions by consensus. No hierarchy. If you can't reach consensus, maybe you need two circles.

**Trauma-informed:** We assume everyone carries trauma. System protects the wounded, not just the "productive."

**Children's voice:** Youth participate in decisions affecting them. Play is infrastructure. Joy is not frivolous.

## FINAL WORDS

**Can you build Flow in informal settlements?**

Maybe. In some contexts. Partially. Over years. With massive adaptation.

**Can you build it with THIS document?**

No. This document is incomplete, outsider-written, and will have major blind spots for your context.

**Then why did we write it?**

Because nothing else addresses the gap between "I want to organize" and "here's how in a context of extreme poverty + violence + climate chaos."

Most development literature is either:
- Too abstract (principles with no practice)
- Too specific (worked in one village, won't generalize)
- Written by outsiders who don't acknowledge limitations

**We tried to:**
- Be concrete enough to be useful
- Be humble enough to not cause harm
- Acknowledge what we don't know
- Ask for correction

**This is version 2.0.**  
**Version 3.0 will have co-authors from informal settlements.**  
**Version 4.0 might not need us at all.**

That's the goal.
