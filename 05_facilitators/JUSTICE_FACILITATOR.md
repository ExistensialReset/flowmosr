## PURPOSE

This guide prepares facilitators to **hold restorative justice processes** under real conditions.

It covers:
- Core skills required
- Common failure modes
- How to recognize and respond to manipulation
- Self-care and burnout prevention
- Minimum training requirements

---

## I. CORE FACILITATOR COMPETENCIES

### 1. STRUCTURE HOLDING

**What it means:**
Maintaining process boundaries when emotions run high.

**Skills:**
- Enforcing speaking order calmly
- Time management (without rigidity)
- Redirecting when off-topic
- Pausing when safety threatened

**Practice:**
- Run practice sessions with experienced facilitators
- Learn to say "I'm pausing this" without apologizing excessively
- Develop neutral language ("Let's return to the structure")

---

### 2. NERVOUS SYSTEM LITERACY

**What it means:**
Reading when someone is dysregulated (fight/flight/freeze/fawn).

**Signs of dysregulation:**
- **Fight:** Raised voice, accusatory language, rigid body
- **Flight:** Leaving, deflecting, topic-switching
- **Freeze:** Silence, shutdown, blank stare
- **Fawn:** Over-apologizing, appeasing, losing own position

**Response:**
- **Pause immediately**
- "I'm noticing some activation. Let's take 2 minutes."
- Offer water, movement, bathroom break
- Do NOT push through dysregulation

**Key principle:**
**No truth can be accessed in fight/flight/freeze.**

---

### 3. MANIPULATION RECOGNITION

**Calm dominance patterns:**
- Person remains composed while others escalate
- Subtly reframes others' statements ("What you really mean is...")
- Expresses concern while maintaining control
- Uses reasonable tone to dismiss concerns

**What to do:**
- Name it neutrally: "I notice you're reframing [name]'s words. Let them speak for themselves."
- Redistribute speaking time explicitly
- Ask direct questions: "What's your own experience, not your interpretation of theirs?"

**High verbal control, low responsiveness:**
- Person talks a lot but doesn't answer questions
- Circles back to their narrative repeatedly
- Evades accountability through complexity

**What to do:**
- Interrupt politely: "I'm going to pause you there."
- Repeat the question clearly
- Enforce time limits strictly

---

### 4. FAIRNESS MONITORING

**What fairness looks like:**
- Equal speaking time (tracked)
- Both parties feel heard
- Process follows stated structure
- No surprise changes mid-session

**What to track:**
- Who has spoken how long (use timer)
- Who got interrupted
- Whose concerns were addressed vs. bypassed

**Self-check:**
- "Am I favoring one person unconsciously?"
- "Have I given both equal space?"
- "Would an observer see this as fair?"

---

### 5. KNOWING YOUR LIMITS

**You are NOT:**
- A therapist
- A judge
- Responsible for fixing people
- Neutral on safety (victim safety > neutrality)

**You ARE:**
- A process guardian
- A structure holder
- Accountable for fairness
- Human and fallible

**When to escalate:**
- You feel out of your depth
- Safety risk you can't manage
- Manipulation you can't name
- Your own nervous system dysregulates

## II. SESSION STRUCTURE (LAYER 2)

### BEFORE SESSION

**Prep (30 min):**
- Review case notes
- Identify potential triggers
- Prepare opening script
- Ensure room is neutral, accessible
- Water available, bathroom nearby

**Safety check:**
- Can both parties be in same room?
- Is physical safety a concern?
- Does anyone need support person?

---

### OPENING (10 min)

**Script template:**

"Welcome. I'm [name], facilitating today. This process is voluntary. You can pause or leave if you need to.

**Ground rules:**
- One person speaks at a time
- No interruptions
- Speak from your own experience ("I felt" not "You always")
- We can pause anytime for regulation

**My role:**
I hold the structure. I'm not here to judge or fix. I'm here to make sure both of you are heard and this process is fair.

**Safety:**
If at any point you feel unsafe, say 'pause' and we stop immediately.

Does anyone have questions before we begin?"

---

### INDIVIDUAL SHARING (20-30 min each)

**Structure:**
- Person A speaks (20 min max)
- Facilitator asks clarifying questions only
- Person B listens (no response yet)
- Switch

**Facilitator prompts:**
- "What happened from your perspective?"
- "What did that feel like for you?"
- "What do you need now?"

**Do NOT:**
- Let Person B interrupt or rebut during A's time
- Allow accusatory language without reframing ("I felt hurt" vs "You're a liar")
- Rush this phase

---

### REFLECTION (10 min)

If Human Mirror present:
- They reflect patterns observed
- Name dynamics neutrally
- Identify misalignments

If no Human Mirror:
- Facilitator reflects key points heard
- Checks: "Did I capture that accurately?"

---

### HARM ARTICULATION (15 min)

**Facilitator asks:**
- "What harm occurred here?"
- "What needs went unmet?"
- "What would repair look like?"

**Guide toward:**
- Specific, actionable needs
- Mutual understanding of impact
- Away from blame/shame spirals

---

### AGREEMENT CREATION (20 min)

**Template:**

"Based on what we've heard, what agreements would help moving forward?

Options might include:
- Behavioral changes
- Communication boundaries
- Support structures
- Timeline for check-in"

**Document lightly:**
- Written agreement (1 page max)
- Signed by both parties
- Copy to each person

---

### CLOSING (5 min)

**Perceived Fairness Protocol:**

"Before we close, I need to ask:

**To [Person A]:** Did this process feel fair to you?  
**To [Person B]:** Did this process feel fair to you?

If no → document why, trigger review.

**Final:**
Thank you both for showing up. This was hard work. You can reach out if you need support processing this."

## III. COMMON FAILURE MODES & RESPONSES

### FAILURE MODE 1: FACILITATOR GETS PULLED INTO CONTENT

**Looks like:**
- Facilitator starts agreeing with one side
- Facilitator offers solutions
- Facilitator debates facts

**How to catch:**
- Notice your own emotional reaction
- Notice if you're talking more than holding structure

**Recovery:**
- Pause
- "I notice I stepped out of my role. Let me return to facilitation."
- Recommit to structure

---

### FAILURE MODE 2: SESSION BECOMES CIRCULAR

**Looks like:**
- Same points repeated
- No movement toward resolution
- Participants exhausted

**Response:**
- Name it: "I notice we're circling. Let's pause."
- Summarize what's been said
- Redirect: "What needs to be different for you to move forward?"

**If still stuck after 2 attempts:**
- End session
- "This needs more time. Let's schedule continuation or escalate to expanded panel."

---

### FAILURE MODE 3: ONE PARTY DOMINATES

**Looks like:**
- One person talks 70%+ of time
- Other person shuts down
- Interruptions allowed to continue

**Response:**
- Interrupt: "I'm going to pause you there."
- "I need to make sure [other person] gets equal time."
- Enforce structure strictly from this point

---

### FAILURE MODE 4: SAFETY RISK EMERGES

**Looks like:**
- Threat of violence (verbal or physical)
- Severe dysregulation (yelling, aggression)
- Someone tries to leave in distress

**Response:**
- **Safety Override Protocol (SOP) immediately**
- "We're stopping. Everyone is safe. We'll reconvene when it's safe."
- Separate parties
- Call for support if needed
- Document incident

## IV. MANIPULATION PATTERNS (DETAILED)

### PATTERN 1: THE REASONABLE ONE

**Behavior:**
- Calm, composed, articulate
- Expresses concern for other party
- Reframes conflict as other's "struggle" or "mental health issue"
- Offers to "help" other party

**Why it works:**
- Facilitator unconsciously aligns with calm person
- Other party appears "unstable" by contrast

**Response:**
- Notice your own bias toward calm = right
- Ask direct questions: "What's your role in this conflict?"
- Track impact, not intent: "What happened regardless of intention?"
- Name pattern if needed: "I notice you're speaking for [other person]. Let them speak."

---

### PATTERN 2: THE VICTIM CLAIMANT

**Behavior:**
- Every question redirected to their own suffering
- Avoids accountability through past trauma
- Uses vulnerability as shield

**Why it works:**
- Genuine empathy from facilitator
- Fear of re-traumatizing

**Response:**
- Hold compassion AND structure
- "I hear that was hard for you. Right now we need to focus on this specific incident."
- "Your experience matters. And so does [other person]'s."
- Separate past trauma from current accountability

---

### PATTERN 3: THE COMPLEXITY GENERATOR

**Behavior:**
- Long, winding explanations
- Introduces tangential details
- Makes everything interconnected
- Evades simple questions

**Why it works:**
- Facilitator gets lost in complexity
- Other party gives up trying to track

**Response:**
- Interrupt: "Let me stop you there."
- Simplify: "In one sentence, what happened?"
- Redirect: "We'll focus on [specific incident]."
- Track time strictly

---

### PATTERN 4: THE RULE FOLLOWER

**Behavior:**
- Performatively adheres to ground rules
- Uses process language to control ("I don't feel safe when...")
- Appeals to fairness while being unfair

**Why it works:**
- Facilitator rewards compliance
- Other party penalized for being less "process-literate"

**Response:**
- Look at impact, not performance
- "You're following the structure. And I notice [other person] isn't getting equal space."
- Track outcomes, not compliance theater

## V. SELF-CARE & BURNOUT PREVENTION

### ROTATION IS MANDATORY

**Rules:**
- Maximum 2 sessions per week
- Minimum 1 week off per month
- No more than 6 months consecutive service

**Why:**
Justice work is emotionally depleting. You cannot sustain this long-term without harm.

---

### SUPERVISION & DEBRIEF

**After each session:**
- 15 min debrief with co-facilitator or mentor
- Journal key dynamics
- Process your own emotional response

**Monthly:**
- Peer supervision (3-5 facilitators)
- Discuss difficult cases
- Learn from each other

---

### SIGNS OF BURNOUT

**Emotional:**
- Cynicism about process
- Irritability with participants
- Feeling numb or detached

**Physical:**
- Exhaustion
- Sleep disruption
- Somatic stress

**Cognitive:**
- Difficulty concentrating
- Second-guessing decisions
- Black/white thinking

**If you notice 3+ signs:**
- Take break immediately
- Inform coordinator
- Access Refugium Anima if needed
- Do NOT push through

---

### WHEN TO STEP BACK

**Immediate:**
- You know parties personally
- You have strong opinion on case
- You're experiencing crisis yourself

**After assessment:**
- Case triggers your own trauma
- You feel unable to maintain neutrality
- You're approaching burnout

**Principle:**
It is honorable to step back. It is harmful to continue when not capable.

## VI. MINIMUM TRAINING REQUIREMENTS

### INITIAL TRAINING (40 hours)

**Module 1: Foundations (8h)**
- Flow Justice Principles
- Trauma-informed practice
- Nervous system basics

**Module 2: Skills (16h)**
- Facilitation techniques
- Active listening
- Boundary setting
- De-escalation

**Module 3: Manipulation (8h)**
- Pattern recognition
- Response strategies
- Role-play exercises

**Module 4: Practice (8h)**
- Supervised mock sessions
- Feedback integration
- Self-care protocols

---

### ONGOING (per year)

**Minimum:**
- 12h peer supervision
- 4h refresher training
- 2h self-care workshop

---

### CHILD WITNESS SPECIALIST (additional 120h)

Required for cases involving children.

## VII. FINAL PRINCIPLES

**You cannot facilitate perfectly.**

You will miss things. You will make mistakes. You will have biases.

**What matters:**
- Commitment to fairness
- Willingness to name your limits
- Consistent structure
- Care for all parties (including yourself)

**The work is:**
- Hold the container
- Protect the process
- Name what you see
- Know when to stop

**Remember:**
Justice is repair, not punishment.  
You are a guardian, not a judge.  
Breath comes before accountability.
