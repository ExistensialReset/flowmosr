## PURPOSE

> "Things that affect kids should be decided WITH kids, not just FOR kids."

**Children's Council** is where kids have real decision-making power over things that matter to them.

**This document explains:**
- What kids can decide
- How decisions are made
- What adults can and can't override
- How it works while living in Mammon (with money)
- How it works in full Flow (without money)

**This protocol is written so KIDS can read and understand it.**

---

## WHAT IS CHILDREN'S COUNCIL?

**Children's Council is a group of kids who make decisions about kid stuff.**

**Examples:**
- What equipment should be on the playground?
- What activities happen at Lyceum Musaeum?
- How should the children's room be decorated?
- What games and toys are available?
- When do kid events happen?

**It's like student council, but with real power, not just suggestions.**

---

## WHO CAN BE IN CHILDREN'S COUNCIL?

### Ages 8-13: Junior Council Members
- Can participate in discussions
- Can vote on kid-specific decisions
- Learn how decision-making works
- **Cannot** be on LOTUS panels yet

### Ages 14-17: Full Council Members
- Can vote on all Council decisions
- Can serve as Council Facilitators (with training)
- Can participate in some Circle/LOTUS decisions
- Have veto power over things affecting kids directly

### Ages 6-7: Observers
- Can attend meetings if they want
- Can share their opinions
- Adults listen to their ideas
- Don't vote yet (too young for formal voting)

### Ages 0-5: Not in Council
- Too young for structured meetings
- Adults represent their needs
- Their opinions heard informally (play, conversation)

## THREE LEVELS OF DECISION POWER

### 🟢 LEVEL 1: KIDS DECIDE (Adults Cannot Override)

**These decisions belong to kids. Adults CANNOT change them.**

**What kids decide:**
- **Playground/play space:**
  - What equipment (swings? climbing wall? sandbox?)
  - Where things go
  - Rules for sharing equipment
  - Who can use what and when

- **Kid spaces:**
  - How children's rooms are decorated
  - What furniture is in kid spaces
  - What toys/games are available
  - Quiet zones vs active zones

- **Lyceum activities:**
  - What classes or workshops kids want
  - What projects to work on
  - Field trips (kids pick destinations from safe options)
  - When events happen (kid dances, game nights, etc.)

- **Kid events:**
  - Birthday party formats
  - Holiday celebrations (how kids celebrate)
  - Sports teams and games
  - Art projects

**Example:**
> **Kids decide:** "We want a climbing wall instead of more swings on the playground."
> 
> **Adults do:** Build the climbing wall (as long as it's safe and within budget).
> 
> **Adults CANNOT say:** "No, we think swings are better."

**Safety exception:** If kids choose something genuinely dangerous, adults can say: "That's not safe. Here are safe alternatives. Pick from these."

---

### 🟡 LEVEL 2: KIDS ADVISE (Adults Listen, But Can Override with Good Reason)

**These decisions affect kids, but also affect adults or the whole community.**

**Kids have STRONG voice. Adults must listen. But adults can override if necessary.**

**What kids advise on:**
- **Circle-wide schedule:**
  - Dinner time
  - Quiet hours
  - Community meeting times

- **Shared spaces:**
  - How common areas are used
  - Noise rules
  - Decoration of shared spaces

- **Community events:**
  - Festivals
  - Gatherings that include kids
  - Travel plans (like Planetary Journey)

- **Food:**
  - Meal preferences (within budget)
  - Snack availability
  - Dietary choices

**Process:**
1. **Kids discuss** in Council
2. **Kids vote** on their preference
3. **Kids present** decision to Circle
4. **Circle listens** (adults must seriously consider)
5. **Circle decides** (can accept, modify, or reject)
6. **If Circle rejects or modifies:** Adults MUST explain why publicly

**Example:**
> **Kids vote:** "Dinner should be at 6pm, not 7pm."
> 
> **Circle considers:** Some adults work until 6:30pm.
> 
> **Circle decides:** "Dinner will be at 6:30pm - compromise between 6pm and 7pm."
> 
> **Circle explains:** "We heard you want earlier dinner. We moved it 30 minutes earlier. We can't do 6pm because some adults aren't home yet. Is 6:30pm okay?"
> 
> **Kids respond:** Accept compromise, or request further discussion.

**Adults CANNOT just ignore kids' input without explanation.**

---

### 🔴 LEVEL 3: ADULTS DECIDE (Kids Protected from This Burden)

**These are adult responsibilities. Kids should NOT have to decide them.**

**What adults decide (kids don't):**
- **Safety rules** (helmets required, no playing near roads, etc.)
- **Baseline provision** (making sure food, housing, healthcare exist)
- **Adult conflicts** (disagreements between grown-ups)
- **Financial management** (in Mammon: paying bills, managing money)
- **Legal compliance** (following laws, dealing with authorities)
- **Serious harm response** (if someone is abusive, dangerous, etc.)

**Why kids don't decide these:**
- Too much responsibility for developing brains
- Adults are accountable if things go wrong
- Kids shouldn't carry stress of adult problems
- Some things require legal authority kids don't have

**Example:**
> **Adult decision:** "We need to fix the roof before winter."
> 
> **Kids are NOT asked:** "Should we pay for the roof or buy new playground equipment?"
> 
> **Why:** Adults are responsible for keeping shelter safe. Kids shouldn't have to choose between safety and fun.

**Note:** Kids CAN give input ("we'd like playground equipment someday"), but don't bear decision burden.

## HOW IT WORKS IN MAMMON (With Money & Budgets)

**While Flow Nodes exist inside Mammon, money is still real.**

**The budget approach:**

### Step 1: Adults Decide Budget
Adults figure out:
- How much money exists for kid stuff
- What's affordable
- What must be paid (safety, maintenance, etc.)

**Example:**
> Circle has $500 for playground improvements this year.
> Circle also has $200 for Lyceum supplies.

### Step 2: Adults Present Budget to Kids
Adults explain:
- "We have $500 for playground."
- "This can buy: climbing wall OR 2 swings + sandbox OR treehouse kit."
- "We have $200 for Lyceum."
- "This can buy: art supplies OR science kits OR musical instruments."

### Step 3: Kids Decide How to Spend It
**Within the budget**, kids have FULL decision power.

**Council meeting:**
- Kids discuss options
- Kids debate pros/cons
- Kids vote
- Winner is implemented

**Example:**
> **Budget:** $500 for playground
> 
> **Options adults researched:**
> - Option A: Climbing wall ($450) + safety mats ($50)
> - Option B: 2 swings ($200) + sandbox ($200) + wood chips ($100)
> - Option C: Treehouse kit ($500)
> 
> **Kids discuss:**
> - "Climbing wall is cool but only 2 kids can use it at once."
> - "Sandbox is for little kids, we're too old."
> - "Treehouse is awesome and lots of us can play in it!"
> 
> **Kids vote:**
> - Climbing wall: 3 votes
> - Swings + sandbox: 2 votes
> - Treehouse: 8 votes
> 
> **Decision:** Treehouse wins!
> 
> **Adults do:** Buy treehouse kit, help kids build it.

### Step 4: Adults Implement
Adults:
- Make the purchase
- Handle logistics (delivery, safety checks, installation)
- Don't override kids' choice (unless safety issue discovered)

**Safety check example:**
> After kids choose treehouse, adults discover it needs to be anchored in concrete, which costs extra $200 not in budget.
> 
> **Adults go back to kids:** "Treehouse needs $200 more for safety. We don't have that this year. Do you want to: (A) wait until next year when we have more budget, or (B) pick a different option from the $500 list?"
> 
> **Kids decide:** Wait or switch - their choice.

### What Kids DON'T Decide in Mammon
- How much money Circle has total (that's adult financial management)
- Whether to pay rent vs buy playground stuff (adults ensure Baseline first)
- Whether to go into debt (adults handle money responsibility)

**Key principle:** Kids decide within budget, adults create budget responsibly.

## HOW IT WORKS IN FULL FLOW (No Money)

**When Flow is established and there's no currency:**

### Adults Still Set Boundaries
- Available labor hours
- Available materials
- Safety requirements
- Feasibility (can we actually build this?)

**Example:**
> **Circle has:**
> - 100 labor-hours available for kid projects this month
> - Wood, rope, and tools in surplus
> - Metal and plastic in moderate supply
> - Concrete in scarcity (RED on scarcity indicators)
> 
> **Adults present to kids:**
> "We can build playground stuff this month. We have plenty of wood and rope. We have some metal. We don't have much concrete. What do you want?"

### Kids Decide Using Flow's Information
Kids learn to check:
- **Resource Impact Scores (RIS):** What does it cost the Earth to make?
- **Scarcity Indicators:** What's available right now?
- **Labor availability:** Who can help build it?

**Council process:**
> **Kids discuss:**
> "Climbing wall needs concrete (scarce). Treehouse needs wood (abundant). Rope swing needs rope (abundant)."
> 
> **Kids consider:**
> "We want climbing wall, but concrete is needed for fixing houses. Maybe we do treehouse and rope swing this time, climbing wall later when concrete is available."
> 
> **Kids vote:**
> Treehouse + rope swing wins!

**Adults implement** using labor-hours and materials.

### What Kids Learn in Flow
- Resources are real (not infinite)
- Choices have trade-offs
- Cooperation makes projects happen
- Delayed gratification sometimes necessary

**But kids still don't carry adult burden** of managing all resources - just making choices within what's available.

## HOW COUNCIL MEETINGS WORK

### Frequency
**Minimum:** Once per month  
**Maximum:** Weekly (if kids want)  
**Special meetings:** Anytime if urgent decision needed

### Who Runs the Meeting?
**Facilitator** - an adult OR teen (14+) who's been trained.

**Facilitator's job:**
- Keep meeting on topic
- Make sure everyone gets to speak
- Help resolve conflicts
- Record decisions
- **NOT** tell kids what to decide

### Meeting Structure (Simple Version)

**1. Welcome (5 minutes)**
- Check in: How is everyone?
- Agenda: What are we deciding today?

**2. Information (10 minutes)**
- Adults present budget OR available resources
- Adults explain options (if any)
- Kids ask clarifying questions

**3. Discussion (15-30 minutes)**
- Kids share opinions
- Kids ask each other questions
- Kids debate pros/cons
- Facilitator makes sure everyone can speak

**4. Proposal (5 minutes)**
- Someone suggests a decision
- "I propose we choose the treehouse."
- Does anyone have a different proposal?

**5. Vote (5 minutes)**
- **Consensus preferred:** Everyone agrees (or stands aside)
- **Majority vote if needed:** More than half must agree
- **Voting methods:**
  - Raise hands
  - Secret ballot (if kids prefer privacy)
  - Dot voting (multiple options, put stickers on favorite)

**6. Decision Recorded (5 minutes)**
- Facilitator writes down what was decided
- Kids confirm: "Is this what we decided?"
- Decision goes to Circle (if Level 2 Advisory)
- Decision implemented immediately (if Level 1 Kids Decide)

**Total time:** 45 minutes - 1 hour (shorter for young kids, longer for complex decisions)

---

## VOTING RULES

### Simple Majority
**Most Council decisions:** More than half of kids present must agree.

**Example:**
- 10 kids at meeting
- 6 vote yes, 4 vote no
- Decision passes (6 is more than half of 10)

### Consensus
**Big decisions (new big projects, changes to Council rules):** Everyone must agree or stand aside.

**Consensus process:**
1. Proposal made
2. Discussion
3. Check for agreement: "Does anyone object?"
4. If objection: Discuss more, modify proposal, try again
5. If no objections (or people "stand aside"): Consensus reached!

**Standing aside:** "I don't love this idea, but I won't block it. Go ahead."

### When a Decision Fails
If vote doesn't pass:
- Discuss more
- Modify proposal
- Come back next meeting
- Or: Drop the idea and move on

**No one is forced to agree.**

## WHAT IF ADULTS IGNORE KIDS' DECISIONS?

### Level 1 (Kids Decide) - Adult Override is NOT ALLOWED

**If adults override a Level 1 decision without safety reason:**

**Step 1:** Kids can formally object to Circle
- Council Facilitator brings complaint to next Circle meeting
- "Council decided treehouse. Adults bought swings instead. Why?"

**Step 2:** Circle must respond
- Explain why override happened
- If no good safety reason: Adults must fix it (build treehouse after all)
- If there IS safety reason: Adults explain and offer alternative

**Step 3:** If Circle ignores or dismisses kids
- Escalate to LOTUS (if Node has LOTUS)
- Escalate to Ombudsperson (external advocate for kids)
- Document in Drift Record (pattern of ignoring kids = red flag)

**Example:**
> **Kids decided:** Treehouse
> 
> **Adults bought:** Swings
> 
> **Reason given:** "We thought swings were safer."
> 
> **This is NOT ACCEPTABLE.** Adults should have presented only safe options to begin with.
> 
> **Resolution:** Adults must sell swings, buy treehouse, or kids choose different option from original safe list.

### Level 2 (Advisory) - Adults Can Override, But Must Explain

**If adults reject kids' advice:**
- Public explanation required
- Must be documented
- Kids can request further discussion
- Circle reviews decision if kids object

**If adults FREQUENTLY ignore kids' input:**
- Pattern becomes visible
- Circle intervention
- Adult education on respecting children's voice
- Facilitator may be removed if repeatedly dismissive

## SPECIAL CASES

### What If Kids Can't Agree?

**If Council is split (close votes, lots of conflict):**

**Option 1: More discussion**
- Take a break
- Come back next meeting
- Let kids think about it

**Option 2: Compromise**
- Find middle ground
- "Can we do half and half?"
- "Can we try one this year, one next year?"

**Option 3: Delayed decision**
- If not urgent, table it
- Come back in a few months
- Maybe preferences will shift

**Option 4: Adults offer tie-breaker only if kids ask**
- Kids can request adult input
- Adults explain their perspective
- Kids make final choice

**Kids are NEVER forced to agree if they genuinely can't.**

---

### What If Only One or Two Kids Show Up?

**Quorum (minimum kids needed):**
- At least 50% of eligible kids
- OR: At least 5 kids (whichever is smaller)

**If quorum not met:**
- Decisions postponed
- Meeting rescheduled
- OR: Non-urgent decisions can proceed with whoever's there (documented as "low turnout decision")

**For major decisions (new big projects):**
- High attendance preferred
- Announcement made ahead of time
- Make sure kids know it's important

---

### What If Adults Try to Influence Kids' Vote?

**This is NOT allowed.**

**Adults can:**
- Present information (budget, safety, options)
- Answer questions honestly
- Explain trade-offs

**Adults CANNOT:**
- Tell kids what to choose
- Pressure or guilt kids
- Threaten consequences if kids choose "wrong"
- Bribe kids with treats/privileges
- Manipulate through fear

**If adult manipulation suspected:**
- Facilitator intervenes
- Private conversation with adult
- If pattern continues: Adult removed from children's spaces
- Reported to Circle

**Example of manipulation:**
> ❌ "If you kids choose treehouse instead of swings, you're being selfish. Little kids need swings."
> 
> ✅ "Swings are good for younger kids. Treehouse is good for older kids. You decide what the priority is."

## INTEGRATION WITH OTHER SYSTEMS

### With Circle Governance
- Council decisions (Level 1) implemented by Circle
- Council advice (Level 2) considered by Circle
- Council represented at Circle meetings (teen member or Facilitator reports)

### With LOTUS (if Node has it)
- Ages 14-17 can serve on LOTUS panels about kid-relevant decisions
- Council can request LOTUS review if Circle ignores them
- LOTUS treats kids' voice seriously (see SMALL_POOL_LOTUS_PROTOCOLS.md)

### With Lyceum Musaeum
- Council decides Lyceum activities, schedule, projects
- Lyceum staff facilitate Council meetings
- Council budget often includes Lyceum supplies

### With SRS (Social Recognition System)
- Kids under 14 NOT in SRS (see CHILDREN_AND_SRS.md)
- Ages 14-17 MAY participate with safeguards
- Council participation itself NOT connected to SRS

## FOR ADULTS: HOW TO SUPPORT COUNCIL

### Do's ✅
- **Respect kids' decisions** (Level 1 is theirs, not yours)
- **Listen to kids' advice** (Level 2 matters, even if you disagree)
- **Be honest about constraints** (budget, safety, feasibility)
- **Facilitate, don't direct** (help process, don't control outcome)
- **Follow through** (if kids decide, make it happen)
- **Explain clearly** (if you must override, say why)
- **Celebrate kids' leadership** (recognize their good decisions)

### Don't's ❌
- **Override Level 1 decisions** (unless genuine safety issue)
- **Ignore Level 2 advice** (without explanation)
- **Manipulate votes** (through pressure, guilt, fear)
- **Dismiss kids' ideas** ("That's silly" or "You're too young to understand")
- **Make kids responsible for adult problems** (financial stress, conflicts)
- **Rush decisions** (let kids take time they need)
- **Punish kids for decisions you don't like** (their choice is their choice)

## FOR KIDS: HOW TO MAKE GOOD DECISIONS

### Tips for Council Members

**1. Listen to everyone**
- Even if you disagree, listen to other kids' ideas
- Everyone's opinion matters

**2. Think about consequences**
- "If we choose this, what happens?"
- "Will everyone get to use it, or just some kids?"

**3. Consider younger kids**
- If you're 13, think about the 6-year-olds too
- Balance needs of different ages

**4. It's okay to change your mind**
- If you learn new information, changing your vote is smart
- "I used to want X, but now I think Y" is fine

**5. Speak up**
- If you have an idea, share it
- If you disagree, say so respectfully

**6. You can ask for help**
- Adults can explain things you don't understand
- Facilitator can help resolve conflicts
- It's okay to say "I don't know" or "I need more information"

**7. Compromise is good**
- Sometimes meeting in the middle is best
- "Can we do both?" or "Can we try one now, one later?"

**8. You can disagree without being mean**
- "I don't think that's a good idea because..." (good)
- "That's stupid and you're dumb" (not okay)

## PRACTICAL EXAMPLES

### Example 1: Playground Equipment (Level 1 - Kids Decide)

**Scenario:**
Circle has $500 for playground. Adults research safe options. Kids decide.

**Process:**
1. **Adults present:** "We have $500. Here are 3 safe options: climbing wall, swings + sandbox, or treehouse. Which do you want?"
2. **Kids discuss:** Pros/cons of each option
3. **Kids vote:** Treehouse wins (8 votes, climbing wall 3, swings 2)
4. **Adults implement:** Buy and help build treehouse
5. **Done!** Kids got what they decided.

---

### Example 2: Dinner Time (Level 2 - Advisory)

**Scenario:**
Kids want earlier dinner. Adults have work constraints.

**Process:**
1. **Kids discuss:** "We're hungry by 6pm, dinner is too late at 7pm."
2. **Kids vote:** "Dinner should be at 6pm" (9 out of 10 kids agree)
3. **Kids present to Circle:** "We vote for 6pm dinner."
4. **Circle considers:** Some adults work until 6:30pm.
5. **Circle decides:** "We'll move dinner to 6:30pm instead of 7pm."
6. **Circle explains:** "We can't do 6pm because some adults aren't home yet. 6:30pm is compromise."
7. **Kids respond:** Accept (it's earlier than before) or request further discussion.

**Outcome:** Kids' voice mattered, decision was adjusted toward their preference, reasoning was transparent.

---

### Example 3: Lyceum Workshop (Level 1 - Kids Decide)

**Scenario:**
Lyceum has 4 hours/week for workshops. Kids choose topics.

**Process:**
1. **Adults offer framework:** "We have 4 hours for workshops. Pick 2 topics from: art, science, music, coding, sports, cooking."
2. **Kids discuss:** What sounds fun?
3. **Kids vote:**
   - Art: 6 votes
   - Science: 8 votes
   - Music: 3 votes
   - Coding: 5 votes
   - Sports: 2 votes
   - Cooking: 4 votes
4. **Top 2:** Science (8) and Art (6)
5. **Decision:** Science workshops and Art workshops this term
6. **Adults facilitate:** Find teachers, gather materials, schedule
7. **Done!** Kids chose, adults made it happen.

---

### Example 4: Kids Disagree About Sharing Rules (Internal Council Decision)

**Scenario:**
Playground has 1 tire swing. Kids disagree about sharing.

**Proposals:**
- **Proposal A:** First come, first served (if you're on it, you can stay as long as you want)
- **Proposal B:** 10-minute turns, then next person
- **Proposal C:** Sign-up sheet

**Discussion:**
- Some kids like Proposal A (freedom)
- Some kids like Proposal B (fairness)
- Some kids think Proposal C is too complicated

**Vote:**
- Proposal A: 3 votes
- Proposal B: 8 votes
- Proposal C: 1 vote

**Decision:** 10-minute turns rule adopted

**Implementation:** Kids enforce it themselves (with adult support if conflicts arise)

**Review:** After 1 month, Council checks if it's working or needs adjustment

## FAILURE MODES & FIXES

### Failure Mode 1: Low Kid Participation

**Problem:** Only 2-3 kids show up to Council meetings.

**Possible causes:**
- Meetings at bad time
- Kids don't think it matters
- Kids too busy
- Meetings boring

**Fixes:**
- Survey kids: "When's a good time?" "Why aren't you coming?"
- Make meetings more engaging (games, snacks, movement breaks)
- Show impact: "Remember when you decided treehouse? It's being built!"
- Shorten meetings if too long
- Make attendance voluntary but show it matters

---

### Failure Mode 2: Adults Overriding Too Often

**Problem:** Circle frequently rejects kids' Level 2 advice or overrides Level 1 decisions.

**Indicators:**
- Kids saying "What's the point, they never listen"
- Council attendance drops
- Kids stop proposing ideas

**Response:**
1. **Circle self-assessment:** "Are we respecting kids' voice?"
2. **Review decisions:** Were overrides justified (safety) or unjustified (adult preference)?
3. **Adjust:** Give kids more autonomy, better budget transparency
4. **Repair:** Apologize if kids were dismissed, implement a delayed kid decision to show trust
5. **If pattern continues:** External review (Ombudsperson, LOTUS)

---

### Failure Mode 3: Older Kids Dominating Younger Kids

**Problem:** 14-year-olds always win votes, 8-year-olds' ideas ignored.

**Indicators:**
- Younger kids stop speaking up
- All decisions favor older kids' interests
- Age-based cliques forming

**Response:**
1. **Facilitator intervention:** "Let's hear from younger kids first."
2. **Structured turn-taking:** Everyone speaks before anyone speaks twice
3. **Age-balanced voting:** For some decisions, vote by age group first, then combine
4. **Separate Junior Council:** Ages 8-10 meet separately sometimes, present ideas to full Council
5. **Adult advocacy:** Adults can say "I notice younger kids want X, how can we include that?"

---

### Failure Mode 4: Kids Choosing Unsustainable/Unfair Options

**Problem:** Kids always choose most expensive/resource-intensive option, or options that only benefit some kids.

**Example:** Council always votes for stuff that only older kids use, younger kids get nothing.

**Response:**
1. **Education:** Help kids understand trade-offs, fairness, sustainability
2. **Framing:** "How can we make sure everyone gets something?"
3. **Review cycle:** Check quarterly: "Are younger kids getting stuff too?"
4. **Budget split:** Allocate budget by age group to ensure fairness
5. **Not punitive:** Don't punish kids for being kids, just guide toward fairness

**Example solution:**
> "We have $500 total. $250 for equipment for ages 6-10, $250 for ages 11-17. Each group decides their half."

## ASSESSMENT QUESTIONS

### For Circle/Node: Is Council Working?

1. Do children have real decision power over things that affect them?
2. Are Level 1 (Kids Decide) decisions implemented without override?
3. Are Level 2 (Advisory) opinions seriously considered?
4. Do kids feel heard and respected?
5. Is participation voluntary and joyful?
6. Are different ages represented in decisions?
7. Do adults facilitate without directing?
8. Are budget constraints clear and honest?
9. Can kids challenge adult decisions that ignore them?
10. Is Council integrated with Circle/LOTUS governance?

**If you can answer "yes" to 8+, Council is working well.**
