## PURPOSE

Children are **never decorative**. When decisions affect them, their voice matters.

This protocol ensures:
- Child perspective represented in justice processes
- Children protected from adult conflict
- Developmental needs centered
- Trauma prevention prioritized

---

## I. CORE PRINCIPLES

### 1. CHILDREN ARE NOT MEDIATORS

**Never:**
- Place child between conflicting adults
- Ask child to "choose sides"
- Use child as messenger
- Expose child to adult conflict resolution

**Always:**
- Represent child's needs through specialist
- Protect child from process details
- Center child's sense of safety

---

### 2. DEVELOPMENTAL APPROPRIATENESS

**Age 0-5:**
- Communication through play, observation
- Focus on felt safety, not verbal articulation
- Parent/caregiver interviews primary

**Age 6-12:**
- Play-based + some verbal communication
- Child can express preferences (where to sleep, who feels safe)
- Specialist translates to adult-comprehensible terms

**Age 13-17:**
- Can participate more directly if they choose
- Still protected from adult conflict details
- Veto power on decisions affecting them

**Age 18+:**
- Adult participant (full voice)

---

### 3. CHILD'S VETO IS ABSOLUTE

On decisions affecting them directly:
- Living arrangements
- Caregiver access
- Safety measures

**If child (via specialist) says "no":**
- Decision does not proceed
- Alternative solutions sought
- Adult preferences subordinate to child safety

---

## II. WHEN CHILD WITNESS ACTIVATES

### MANDATORY ACTIVATION

**When:**
- Both parents/caregivers involved in conflict
- Conflict affects child's daily life (housing, routines, safety)
- Child has expressed distress (to anyone)
- Decisions will impact child directly

**Examples:**
- Co-parenting disputes
- Household conflict where child lives
- Caregiver accused of harm to child
- Resource allocation affecting child's needs

---

### OPTIONAL ACTIVATION

**When:**
- Child peripherally affected by adult conflict
- Community decision impacts children broadly
- Educational/care structure changes

**Process:**
- Facilitator consults with Child Witness Specialist
- Specialist assesses relevance
- Activates if child perspective needed

---

## III. CHILD WITNESS SPECIALIST ROLE

### QUALIFICATIONS

**Minimum training:** 120 hours

**Required modules:**
- Child development (40h)
- Trauma-informed child engagement (30h)
- Play-based assessment (20h)
- Ethics and boundaries (15h)
- Documentation and reporting (15h)

**Plus:**
- Background check (child safety)
- Supervised practice (20 sessions)
- Ongoing supervision (monthly)

---

### RESPONSIBILITIES

**Before process:**
1. Meet with child separately (safe space, no adults)
2. Assess child's understanding of situation
3. Identify child's needs/fears/preferences
4. Translate to adult-legible language

**During process:**
5. Represent child's perspective (child NOT present)
6. Clarify when adult decisions conflict with child needs
7. Enforce child's veto if applicable

**After process:**
8. Meet with child again (explain outcomes age-appropriately)
9. Check in on child's sense of safety
10. Document child's experience

---

### WHAT SPECIALIST SAYS

**Example phrasings:**

"The child experiences this conflict as unpredictability. They need to know where they will sleep and that both parents are safe."

"The child has expressed fear of [person]. Until that fear is addressed, proximity to [person] is not safe for this child."

"The child needs routine. These adults' conflict is disrupting meals, bedtime, and school. The child needs stability restored immediately."

---

### WHAT SPECIALIST DOES NOT SAY

**Never:**
- "The child said [exact quote]" (protects child from being target)
- "The child blames [person]" (avoids scapegoating language)
- "The child is fine" (if child is not fine)

**Always:**
- Represent child's *experience* and *needs*
- Protect child's confidentiality
- Center safety over adult preferences

---

## IV. CHILD ASSESSMENT PROCESS

### LOCATION

**Neutral, child-friendly space:**
- Toys, art supplies, comfortable seating
- NOT parent's home (unless only safe option)
- NOT justice session room

---

### APPROACH

**Play-based for young children:**
- Dolls/figures for role-play
- Drawing materials ("Draw your family")
- Storytelling ("Tell me about a time you felt safe/scared")

**Conversational for older children:**
- Open questions ("How do you feel when [situation]?")
- Multiple choice (easier than open-ended for some)
- Validation ("It makes sense you'd feel that way")

---

### WHAT TO ASSESS

**Safety:**
- Who feels safe to the child?
- What situations cause fear?
- Physical safety vs. emotional safety

**Stability:**
- Does child know where they'll sleep tonight?
- Is routine predictable?
- Are basic needs met?

**Relationships:**
- Who does child want access to?
- Who does child want distance from?
- What does "good" contact look like?

**Needs:**
- What does child need to feel okay?
- What would make things better?

---

### RED FLAGS (IMMEDIATE ACTION)

**If child discloses:**
- Physical abuse
- Sexual abuse
- Neglect (food, shelter, medical)
- Suicidal ideation

**Response:**
1. Do NOT investigate further (that's not specialist's role)
2. Ensure child is safe NOW
3. Report to authorities as mandated
4. Inform facilitator (case escalates to protective measures)

---

## V. INTEGRATION WITH JUSTICE PROCESS

### BEFORE SESSION

**Specialist briefs facilitator:**
- Child's key needs
- Safety concerns
- Veto items (if any)

---

### DURING SESSION

**Specialist attends (child does NOT):**
- Sits separately from parties
- Speaks when child's needs relevant
- Can interrupt if child safety threatened by discussion

**Example intervention:**

Adult 1: "I think the child should spend equal time with both of us."

Specialist: "I need to pause here. The child has expressed fear of [situation in Adult 1's home]. Equal time is not safe until that's addressed."

---

### CLOSING SESSION

**Specialist ensures:**
- Agreement protects child's baseline (food, shelter, safety)
- Child's stated preferences honored (or documented why not)
- Follow-up plan includes check-in with child

---

## VI. FOLLOW-UP WITH CHILD

### TIMING

**Within 3 days of justice process**

---

### APPROACH

**Age-appropriate explanation:**

"The grown-ups had a meeting about how to make things better. Here's what they decided: [simple, concrete explanation].

This means for you: [specific impact on child's life].

How do you feel about that?"

---

### IF CHILD UNHAPPY WITH OUTCOME

**Response:**
"I hear you. I'm going to make sure the grown-ups know how you feel. Your voice matters."

**Action:**
- Document child's response
- Report to facilitator
- Trigger review if child's safety concerns unresolved

---

## VII. EDGE CASES

### CASE 1: CHILD WANTS TO PARTICIPATE DIRECTLY

**If child 13+:**
- Possible if:
  - Child explicitly requests it
  - Specialist assesses child can handle process
  - Session structure adapted (shorter, gentler)
  
**If child refuses specialist representation:**
- Child's choice respected
- But: Child still protected from retaliation
- Follow-up mandatory

---

### CASE 2: BOTH PARENTS DISPUTE SPECIALIST'S REPRESENTATION

**Response:**
"The specialist is not representing a parent. They're representing the child's needs. If you disagree with what the child needs, we address that. But we don't dismiss the child's voice."

**Specialist's authority:**
- On child safety: Specialist's assessment stands
- On child preferences: Can be discussed but not overridden without good reason

---

### CASE 3: CHILD TOO YOUNG TO ASSESS (INFANT)

**Focus:**
- Observable needs (fed, warm, held)
- Caregiver capacity to meet needs
- Stability of environment

**Specialist role:**
- Advocate for baseline (even if child can't speak)
- Assess caregivers, not child directly

---

### CASE 4: CHILD COACHED BY PARENT

**Signs:**
- Child uses adult language
- Child's account mirrors parent's exactly
- Child contradicts self when away from parent

**Response:**
- Multiple sessions (separate from parents)
- Play-based assessment (harder to coach)
- Document inconsistencies
- Escalate if coaching is manipulative

**Principle:**
Even if child is coached, child's fear is real. Address the fear, investigate the coaching separately.

---

## VIII. SPECIALIST SELF-CARE

### LOAD LIMITS

**Maximum:**
- 3 active cases at once
- 10 assessments per month

**Mandatory:**
- 1 week off per quarter
- Monthly supervision

---

### EMOTIONAL TOLL

**This work is hard because:**
- Children's pain is visceral
- You cannot fix everything
- You hold knowledge of harm

**Required:**
- Access to Refugium Anima
- Peer support group (other specialists)
- Therapy/supervision external to Flow

---

### WHEN TO STEP BACK

**Immediate:**
- Case triggers your own childhood trauma
- You feel unable to stay neutral between parents
- You're experiencing vicarious trauma symptoms

**It is not weak to step back. It is responsible.**

---

## IX. DOCUMENTATION

### WHAT TO DOCUMENT

**From child assessment:**
- Child's stated needs (paraphrased, not quoted)
- Observed affect (happy, scared, shut down)
- Safety concerns
- Preferences

**NOT:**
- Identifying details unnecessary for case
- Exact quotes (unless disclosure of abuse)
- Specialist's personal opinions

---

### STORAGE

**Confidential:**
- Encrypted
- Access limited to specialist + case facilitator
- Deleted after 90 days (unless ongoing case)

**Never:**
- Shared with non-involved parties
- Used for purposes outside this case
- Retained permanently

---

## XI. FINAL PRINCIPLES

**Children are not small adults.**

Their brains are still developing. Their capacity to articulate is limited. Their sense of time, causality, and safety is different.

**What this means:**

- We don't ask children to solve adult problems
- We don't expect children to "get over it" quickly
- We don't prioritize adult convenience over child safety

**The specialist exists to:**

- Translate child's experience into adult-legible needs
- Protect child from process that could harm them
- Ensure child's voice is heard even when they can't speak for themselves

**Remember:**

Children don't choose their families.  
Children don't choose their conflicts.  
Children deserve safety regardless of adult behavior.

**This is non-negotiable.**
