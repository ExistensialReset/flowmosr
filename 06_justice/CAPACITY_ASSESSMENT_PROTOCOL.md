## PURPOSE

**When someone causes harm during mental health crisis, Flow must answer:**
- Could they understand their actions at the time?
- Could they control their behavior?
- What level of accountability is appropriate?

**This is NOT about:**
- "Guilty" vs "innocent" (Flow doesn't use these terms)
- "Sane" vs "insane" (outdated, stigmatizing)
- "Excusing" harm (even with impaired capacity, repair still needed)

**This IS about:**
- Appropriate response to harm (Level 1, 2, or 3?)
- What support does person need? (clinical vs justice focus)
- How to restore safety for victim AND respond to perpetrator's needs?

**Flow's position:**  
**Capacity affects HOW accountability happens, not WHETHER it happens.**

---

## I. FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES

### 1.1 CAPACITY IS CONTEXTUAL, NOT BINARY

**Traditional model:**
- "Sane" = fully accountable
- "Insane" = not accountable at all

**Flow model:**
- Capacity exists on spectrum
- Can be present in some domains, absent in others
- Fluctuates over time and context
- Assessment is about DEGREE, not yes/no

**Example:**
Person in psychotic episode:
- May understand "I hit someone" (factual awareness)
- May NOT understand "This will hurt them" (empathy/consequence awareness)
- Capacity is PARTIAL → accountability is ADAPTED

---

### 1.2 IMPAIRED CAPACITY ≠ NO ACCOUNTABILITY

**What impaired capacity means in Flow:**
- Person may not be appropriate for Level 1 Restorative Circle (if they can't engage meaningfully)
- Person may need Level 2 (separation + treatment) instead of Level 1 (dialogue)
- Person's Baseline maintained (never suspended for lack of capacity)
- Victim still protected (interim measures still apply)

**What impaired capacity does NOT mean:**
- "Person is excused, victim must just accept"
- "No consequences because not their fault"
- "System does nothing"

**Restoration still needed. Just adapted to person's capacity.**

---

### 1.3 TRAUMA-INFORMED ASSESSMENT

**Traditional psychiatric evaluation:**
- Focus on diagnosis (what disorder?)
- Focus on risk (will they do it again?)
- Focus on legal culpability (punishable or not?)

**Flow capacity assessment:**
- Focus on current functioning (what can they understand NOW?)
- Focus on support needs (what help needed to restore capacity?)
- Focus on safety (victim AND perpetrator)
- Trauma history considered (how did capacity become impaired?)

**Assessment is SUPPORTIVE, not PUNITIVE.**

---

### 1.4 NERVOUS SYSTEM FIRST

**From FLOW_JUSTICE_PRINCIPLES:**
> "Justice moves at the speed of the nervous system."

**Capacity cannot be assessed when person is:**
- In active crisis (psychosis, mania, severe dissociation)
- Dysregulated (fight/flight/freeze)
- Under influence (substances, medication side effects)

**Process:**
1. Stabilize person (Refugium Anima, medical care if needed)
2. Allow nervous system to regulate (days to weeks)
3. THEN assess capacity
4. THEN determine appropriate justice response

**No rushing. Safety and accuracy over speed.**

---

## II. WHO CONDUCTS ASSESSMENTS

### 2.1 PRIMARY ASSESSOR: LICENSED MENTAL HEALTH PROFESSIONAL

**Qualifications required:**
- Licensed psychologist, psychiatrist, or clinical social worker
- Minimum 3 years experience with trauma and mental health crises
- Training in Flow capacity assessment protocols (20 hours)
- Understanding of Flow justice principles (not traditional legal concepts)

**Why professional required:**
- Capacity assessment requires clinical expertise
- Legal liability if assessment wrong
- Credibility (both for victim and for perpetrator)

**Where professionals come from:**
- **Node level:** If Node has mental health professional, they conduct
- **Regional level:** If Node lacks professional, regional pool provides assessor
- **External:** If conflict of interest (assessor knows perpetrator personally), external professional brought in

---

### 2.2 SUPPORTING ROLES

**In addition to primary assessor:**

**Human Mirror (present during assessment):**
- Ensures process follows Flow principles
- Protects perpetrator from invasive/punitive questioning
- Provides context (knows person's history in Node)
- NOT clinician, but trained facilitator

**Victim advocate (if victim wants):**
- Ensures victim's safety concerns heard
- May provide information about incident
- Does NOT attend assessment itself (perpetrator's privacy protected)

**Medical professional (if needed):**
- Neurologist if brain injury suspected
- Physician if physical health affecting capacity
- Pharmacist if medication effects relevant

**Cultural liaison (if relevant):**
- If perpetrator from culture where mental health interpreted differently
- Provides cultural context for assessment
- Ensures culturally appropriate communication

---

### 2.3 PEER REVIEW REQUIREMENT

**No single assessor decides alone.**

**All capacity assessments reviewed by:**
- Second mental health professional (peer review)
- Human Mirror (for Flow principle compliance)
- If disagreement: Third professional consulted (tiebreaker)

**Why peer review:**
- Prevents bias (personal, cultural, professional)
- Catches errors (misdiagnosis, misunderstanding)
- Increases credibility (both for victim and perpetrator)

**Timeline:**
- Primary assessment: 7-14 days
- Peer review: 7 days
- Total: 14-21 days for complete assessment

---

## III. ASSESSMENT PROCESS

### 3.1 INITIAL STABILIZATION (BEFORE ASSESSMENT)

**Immediately after incident:**

**Step 1: Safety**
- Victim separated from perpetrator (interim measures)
- Perpetrator provided Baseline (housing, food, medical care)
- If medical emergency: Hospital care immediately

**Step 2: Crisis response**
- If perpetrator in active crisis: Refugium Anima or medical stabilization
- No interrogation during crisis
- Focus on nervous system regulation

**Step 3: Medication review**
- What medications is person on?
- Did they take them as prescribed?
- Are side effects affecting capacity?
- Medication adjustment if needed (with consent)

**Step 4: Basic needs**
- Sleep (protected, quiet space)
- Food and water
- Temperature comfort
- Safety from self-harm

**Timeline: 3-7 days minimum before formal assessment begins.**

**Person cannot be accurately assessed while dysregulated.**

---

### 3.2 CAPACITY ASSESSMENT INTERVIEW

**Conducted by licensed mental health professional + Human Mirror present.**

**Setting:**
- Private, quiet, non-threatening space
- Perpetrator not restrained (unless immediate danger)
- Refugium-like environment (calm, supportive)
- 60-90 minutes typical duration
- Can be split across multiple sessions if person fatigues

**Structure:**

#### PART A: FACTUAL AWARENESS (15-20 minutes)

**Questions asked:**
- "Do you remember what happened on [date]?"
- "Can you describe what you did?"
- "Who was involved?"
- "Where were you?"

**Assessor evaluates:**
- Can person recall events? (memory intact or impaired?)
- Is recall coherent? (organized narrative or fragmented?)
- Does person acknowledge actions? (denial or recognition?)

**Capacity indicators:**
- **Full capacity:** Clear, coherent recall of events
- **Partial capacity:** Fragmented memory, gaps, confusion
- **Impaired capacity:** No memory, complete dissociation, false memories

---

#### PART B: UNDERSTANDING OF IMPACT (20-30 minutes)

**Questions asked:**
- "How do you think [victim] felt during this?"
- "What do you think happened to them afterward?"
- "If someone did this to you, how would you feel?"
- "Do you understand why [victim] might be afraid/angry/hurt?"

**Assessor evaluates:**
- Can person recognize victim's perspective? (theory of mind functioning?)
- Does person show empathy? (emotional connection or flat affect?)
- Can person understand consequences? (cause-effect reasoning?)

**Capacity indicators:**
- **Full capacity:** Recognizes victim's pain, shows appropriate concern
- **Partial capacity:** Understands intellectually but lacks emotional connection
- **Impaired capacity:** Cannot grasp victim's perspective, no empathy, confused about consequences

---

#### PART C: CONTROL OVER BEHAVIOR (15-20 minutes)

**Questions asked:**
- "Before you [acted], what were you feeling?"
- "Did you think about what you were doing?"
- "Could you have stopped yourself?"
- "Have you done this before? Why this time?"

**Assessor evaluates:**
- Was action impulsive or deliberate?
- Did person have warning signs? (internal awareness?)
- Could person have used alternative? (access to self-regulation?)
- Was behavior driven by symptoms? (hallucinations, delusions, mania?)

**Capacity indicators:**
- **Full capacity:** Person had awareness, made choice (even if poor choice)
- **Partial capacity:** Person felt compelled but had some awareness
- **Impaired capacity:** No awareness, no control, symptoms driving behavior entirely

---

#### PART D: CURRENT FUNCTIONING (15-20 minutes)

**Questions asked:**
- "How are you feeling right now?"
- "What do you think should happen next?"
- "Do you understand why we're having this conversation?"
- "Can you think clearly, or is your mind still foggy/confused?"

**Assessor evaluates:**
- Is person currently stable? (crisis resolved or ongoing?)
- Can person engage in dialogue? (ready for restorative process?)
- Does person have insight? (recognizes they acted harmfully?)
- What support does person need? (clinical, social, structural?)

**Capacity indicators:**
- **Full capacity:** Stable, insightful, can engage in restorative process
- **Partial capacity:** Stabilizing, developing insight, needs ongoing support
- **Impaired capacity:** Still unstable, limited insight, needs extended treatment before restoration possible

---

### 3.3 CLINICAL OBSERVATION

**In addition to interview, assessor observes:**

**Cognitive functioning:**
- Coherence of speech (organized or disorganized?)
- Memory (intact or impaired?)
- Attention (focused or scattered?)
- Reasoning (logical or delusional?)

**Emotional functioning:**
- Affect (appropriate, flat, labile?)
- Mood (stable, depressed, manic?)
- Empathy (present or absent?)
- Remorse (genuine, performative, absent?)

**Behavioral indicators:**
- Eye contact (engaged or avoidant?)
- Body language (open or defensive?)
- Cooperation (willing or resistant?)
- Insight (recognizes problem or denies?)

**Somatic signs:**
- Tremors, tics, or involuntary movements (neurological?)
- Psychomotor agitation or retardation (mood disorder?)
- Self-harm indicators (scars, fresh wounds?)
- Signs of substance use (withdrawal, intoxication residue?)

---

### 3.4 COLLATERAL INFORMATION

**Assessor also gathers:**

**From medical records:**
- Psychiatric diagnoses (if any)
- Medication history
- Previous hospitalizations or crises
- Brain injury, neurological conditions

**From Node members (with consent):**
- How was person functioning before incident? (baseline)
- Any warning signs observed? (escalating symptoms)
- Pattern of behavior? (first time or recurring)
- Social support available? (isolated or connected?)

**From victim (if willing):**
- What did perpetrator say during incident? (content of speech)
- How did perpetrator seem? (affect, behavior)
- Any context victim noticed? (triggers, stressors)

**Balance:**
- Victim's account important BUT not definitive
- Person's behavior during crisis may not reflect capacity during crisis (paradox: seeming "normal" while dissociated)
- Multiple sources create more accurate picture

## IV. CAPACITY DETERMINATION

### 4.1 CAPACITY LEVELS (NOT DIAGNOSES)

**Flow uses capacity levels, not psychiatric diagnoses.**

**Why:**
- Diagnosis tells what disorder person has
- Capacity tells what person could understand/control
- Same diagnosis (e.g., schizophrenia) can have different capacity levels depending on symptom severity, treatment, functioning

**Three capacity levels:**

---

#### LEVEL A: FULL CAPACITY

**Definition:**
Person had substantial ability to understand their actions and control their behavior at time of incident.

**Indicators:**
- Clear memory of events
- Understood victim would be harmed
- Had some degree of choice (even if impaired judgment)
- Was not driven entirely by symptoms (delusions, hallucinations)

**Justice response:**
- Level 1 Restorative Circle appropriate (if minor harm)
- Level 3 LOTUS review (if severe harm)
- Standard accountability processes apply
- Mental health support offered (but not substitute for accountability)

**Example:**
Person with depression gets angry, hits someone. They knew what they were doing, chose to act, regret it now. Diagnosis exists but capacity was intact.

---

#### LEVEL B: PARTIAL CAPACITY

**Definition:**
Person had some understanding of actions but limited ability to control behavior due to mental health symptoms.

**Indicators:**
- Fragmented memory or impaired awareness
- Some recognition of harm but limited empathy
- Behavior partially driven by symptoms (e.g., paranoid beliefs influencing actions)
- Would have been difficult (but not impossible) to stop

**Justice response:**
- Level 2 Protective Coexistence likely appropriate
- Separation + intensive treatment
- Restorative dialogue possible AFTER stabilization (months later)
- Focus on nervous system regulation, symptom management, capacity restoration
- Victim protected through distance, not through punishment of perpetrator

**Example:**
Person in manic episode becomes aggressive, doesn't fully recognize danger to others. Some awareness present but judgment severely impaired. Treatment needed before restoration possible.

---

#### LEVEL C: IMPAIRED CAPACITY

**Definition:**
Person had minimal to no ability to understand actions or control behavior due to severe mental health crisis.

**Indicators:**
- No memory of incident (complete dissociation) OR false memories (psychotic confusion)
- No understanding of impact on victim (severe empathy deficit due to symptoms)
- Behavior entirely driven by symptoms (command hallucinations, acute psychosis, dissociative fugue)
- No volitional control (couldn't have stopped even if they wanted to)

**Justice response:**
- Level 2 with extended clinical focus (not Level 1, not Level 3)
- Medical stabilization primary goal
- Victim protected through separation
- Restorative dialogue ONLY if/when person regains capacity (could be many months/years)
- If capacity never fully returns: Permanent protective coexistence (separation without punishment)

**Example:**
Person in acute psychotic break attacks someone believing they're a demon. No memory of event afterward. Was experiencing command hallucinations. Medical emergency, not accountability case.

---

### 4.2 BORDERLINE CASES (AMBIGUOUS CAPACITY)

**What if capacity is unclear?**

**Two assessors disagree:**
- Third assessor consulted (tiebreaker)
- If still unclear: Default to LOWER capacity level (protective stance)
- Rationale: Better to under-estimate accountability than over-estimate when mental health involved

**Capacity fluctuated during incident:**
- Some moments person seemed aware, others dissociated
- Assessment based on LOWEST capacity during incident
- If person had clear moments, partial capacity assigned

**Cultural differences in expression:**
- Symptoms may manifest differently across cultures
- Cultural liaison consulted
- Assessment accounts for cultural context (e.g., trance states, spiritual experiences)

**Malingering concerns:**
- Is person faking symptoms to avoid accountability?
- Assessor uses clinical judgment + peer review
- If malingering suspected: Full capacity assigned
- But rare (people genuinely in crisis don't typically "fake" to this degree)

## V. DOCUMENTATION & REPORTING

### 5.1 ASSESSMENT REPORT CONTENTS

**Written report includes:**

**Section 1: Background**
- Incident summary (what happened, when, who involved)
- Person's mental health history
- Medication status
- Collateral information sources

**Section 2: Clinical Findings**
- Interview observations
- Cognitive/emotional/behavioral functioning
- Somatic indicators
- Diagnostic impressions (if relevant)

**Section 3: Capacity Determination**
- Level A, B, or C (with rationale)
- Specific capacities present/absent (memory, empathy, control)
- Contextual factors (stressors, triggers, supports)

**Section 4: Recommendations**
- Appropriate justice response (Level 1, 2, or 3)
- Treatment needs (medication, therapy, Refugium)
- Safety considerations (for victim and perpetrator)
- Timeline for reassessment (if capacity may improve)

**Section 5: Dissenting Opinions (if any)**
- If peer reviewer disagrees, their perspective included
- Helps LOTUS understand complexity

**Length:** 3-5 pages typical, plain language alongside clinical terms

---

### 5.2 WHO RECEIVES REPORT

**Report shared with:**

**Required:**
- Human Mirrors facilitating case
- LOTUS panel (if Level 3 case)
- Peer reviewer(s)

**Optional (with consent):**
- Perpetrator themselves (can read their own assessment)
- Perpetrator's support person (family, advocate)
- Treating clinician (for continuity of care)

**NOT shared with:**
- Victim (unless perpetrator consents AND assessor judges it won't compromise victim safety)
- General Node population (privacy protected)
- External parties (unless legal requirement)

**Why limited sharing:**
- Protects perpetrator's medical privacy
- Prevents stigma ("they're crazy")
- Maintains focus on restoration, not labeling

---

### 5.3 STORAGE & CONFIDENTIALITY

**Report stored:**
- Encrypted (secure file)
- Accessible only to authorized parties (Human Mirrors, LOTUS if relevant)
- Deleted after 5 years if no repeat incidents (decay principle)

**If repeat incident:**
- Previous assessment may be referenced (pattern consideration)
- But each incident assessed independently (capacity can change over time)

**Perpetrator's right:**
- Can request deletion of report (except if ongoing justice process)
- Can request amendment if they believe report inaccurate (peer review process)

## VI. INTEGRATION WITH JUSTICE SYSTEM

### 6.1 WHEN CAPACITY ASSESSMENT OCCURS

**Timeline in justice process:**

**Day 0-3:** Incident occurs, immediate safety response
- Victim separated from perpetrator
- Perpetrator offered Refugium or medical care
- NO capacity assessment yet (too soon)

**Day 3-7:** Stabilization
- Person's nervous system regulates
- Medical needs addressed
- No formal assessment yet (still stabilizing)

**Day 7-14:** Capacity assessment begins
- Assessor interviews person
- Collateral information gathered
- Peer review conducted

**Day 14-21:** Assessment complete
- Report finalized
- Shared with Human Mirrors/LOTUS
- Justice response determined

**Day 21+:** Justice process proceeds
- Level 1, 2, or 3 based on capacity + severity
- Treatment concurrent with justice (not instead of)

**Key:** Assessment happens EARLY but not IMMEDIATELY. Balance accuracy with timeliness.

---

### 6.2 HOW CAPACITY AFFECTS JUSTICE LEVEL

**Capacity + Harm Severity = Justice Response**

| Capacity | Minor Harm | Moderate Harm | Severe Harm |
|----------|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Full (Level A)** | Level 1 (Restorative Circle) | Level 1 or 2 (depending) | Level 3 (LOTUS review) |
| **Partial (Level B)** | Level 1 adapted OR Level 2 | Level 2 (separation + treatment) | Level 2 with intensive support |
| **Impaired (Level C)** | Level 2 (clinical focus) | Level 2 (extended treatment) | Level 2 (possibly permanent if capacity not restored) |

**Key insights:**
- Even FULL capacity + minor harm = Level 1 (capacity doesn't make minor harm major)
- Impaired capacity + severe harm ≠ Level 3 (because person can't engage in LOTUS meaningfully)
- Partial capacity = most common, requires adapted response

---

### 6.3 ADAPTED RESTORATIVE PROCESSES

**If person has partial capacity:**

**Modifications to Level 1 Restorative Circle:**
- More time for person to process (slow pace)
- Breaks as needed (nervous system regulation)
- Simplified language (avoid complex abstractions)
- Support person present (therapist or advocate)
- Focus on concrete repair (not abstract accountability)

**Example:**
Person with intellectual disability caused harm. Understands "I hurt someone" but can't articulate complex empathy. Restorative circle adapts: uses simple language, focuses on concrete actions ("You will help fix what you broke"), provides support.

---

### 6.4 REASSESSMENT PROTOCOLS

**Capacity can change over time.**

**Reassessment triggered by:**
- Significant time elapsed (6+ months)
- Treatment completed (medications stabilized, therapy progress)
- Person requests (believes capacity has improved)
- Victim requests (wants to attempt dialogue, needs to know if person is stable)

**Process:**
- Same rigor as initial assessment
- New assessor (if possible, to avoid bias)
- Comparison to initial assessment (has capacity improved, worsened, stayed same?)
- New recommendations

**If capacity improved:**
- Person may now be appropriate for restorative dialogue
- Level 2 may transition to Level 1 process
- BUT: Victim veto still applies (victim may not want dialogue even if person now capable)

**If capacity worsened or unchanged:**
- Continue Level 2 (separation + treatment)
- Focus on quality of life for person, safety for victim
- Accept that restoration may not be possible

## VII. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

### 7.1 CHILDREN & ADOLESCENTS

**Capacity assessment for youth (under 25):**

**Developmental considerations:**
- Brain not fully developed (especially prefrontal cortex for impulse control)
- Capacity naturally lower than adults
- Accountability adapted to developmental stage (see YOUTH_HARM_PROTOCOL.md)

**Assessment modifications:**
- Child development specialist involved
- Age-appropriate language and questions
- Parent/guardian consent required (but child's voice prioritized)
- Trauma history heavily weighted (many youth in justice system are trauma survivors)

**Default assumption:**
**Youth have reduced capacity compared to adults, even without mental health diagnosis.**

**Justice response:**
- Almost never Level 3 LOTUS for youth
- Focus on Level 1 adapted (restorative with support) or Level 2 (treatment + education)
- Restoration primary goal, punishment never

---

### 7.2 NEURODIVERGENT INDIVIDUALS

**Autism, ADHD, intellectual disability, etc.:**

**Capacity assessment considerations:**
- Neurodivergence ≠ mental health crisis (separate categories)
- Many neurodivergent people have FULL capacity (just different processing)
- Assessment must not conflate neurodivergence with incapacity

**Red flags (assessor biases to avoid):**
- "They don't make eye contact" → NOT indicator of lack of empathy (autism)
- "They can't sit still" → NOT indicator of lack of control (ADHD)
- "They process slowly" → NOT indicator of lack of understanding (intellectual disability)

**Adapted assessment:**
- Neurodivergent-affirming communication (written, visual supports, extended time)
- Assessor trained in neurodiversity
- Focus on actual understanding, not neurotypical performance

**Example:**
Autistic person caused harm in meltdown. Capacity assessment finds: understood actions intellectually, but sensory overload prevented control. Capacity partial, not impaired. Response: Sensory accommodations + adapted restorative process.

---

### 7.3 ELDERLY WITH DEMENTIA

**Progressive cognitive decline:**

**Capacity assessment challenges:**
- Dementia may eliminate capacity entirely
- Person may not remember incident at all
- Accountability becomes clinical care, not justice

**Justice response:**
- Level 2 focus (medical care, separation for safety)
- No restorative circle (person can't engage)
- Victim still protected (distance maintained)
- Caregiver support (family may be overwhelmed, need Refugium)

**Philosophical question:**
**If person has no capacity, is it "justice" or just "care"?**

**Flow's position:**
- Justice = restoration of Flow (calm, safety, dignity)
- Care = form of restoration when dialogue impossible
- So yes, care IS justice in this context

**See:** FLOW_JUSTICE_PRINCIPLES.md Scenario 4 (Elder with Dementia)

---

### 7.4 SUBSTANCE-RELATED CAPACITY

**Intoxication vs. addiction vs. dual diagnosis:**

**Intoxication during incident:**
- If voluntarily intoxicated: Capacity generally FULL (person chose to use substance)
- If involuntarily intoxicated: Capacity IMPAIRED (drugged without knowledge)
- Chronic substance use may impair baseline capacity (brain damage from long-term use)

**Addiction as factor:**
- Addiction is health condition, not moral failing
- Capacity assessment considers: Could person have NOT used? (compulsion vs. choice)
- If addiction driving behavior: Partial capacity likely
- Treatment concurrent with justice process

**Example:**
Person with alcohol addiction hits someone while drunk. Assessment: Full capacity (chose to drink, knew consequences). BUT: Treatment needed alongside accountability (Level 1 + substance treatment program).

---

## VIII. APPEALS & DISPUTES

### 8.1 PERPETRATOR DISAGREES WITH ASSESSMENT

**If person believes assessment wrong:**

**Process:**
1. Request second opinion (different assessor)
2. Second assessor conducts independent evaluation
3. If significant disagreement: Third assessor (tiebreaker)
4. If still disputed: LOTUS review

**LOTUS considers:**
- All three assessments
- Original incident details
- Person's functioning over time
- Victim's input (if willing)

**LOTUS decides:**
- Capacity level (A, B, or C)
- Appropriate justice response
- Decision is FINAL

**Timeline:** 60 days for full appeal process

---

### 8.2 VICTIM DISAGREES WITH ASSESSMENT

**If victim believes assessment wrong (e.g., thinks person had more capacity than assessed):**

**Victim's concern:**
"They're faking. They knew what they were doing. This is just an excuse."

**Response:**
1. Victim's concern taken seriously (not dismissed)
2. Assessor explains reasoning (in plain language, respects victim's perspective)
3. If victim still disagrees: Peer review process examines for assessor bias
4. If assessment stands: Victim supported in their feelings (validation, Refugium access)

**Important:**
- Victim does NOT get to override clinical assessment
- But victim's safety concerns DO inform justice response (even if capacity impaired, separation maintained)
- Victim veto power applies to re-entry, not to capacity determination

**Example:**
Assessor finds impaired capacity. Victim insists person was "playing crazy." Peer review confirms original assessment. Response: "We understand your anger. The assessment stands. And: perpetrator will remain separated from you indefinitely, even though capacity impaired. Your safety is paramount."

---

### 8.3 ASSESSOR BIAS OR ERROR

**If assessment appears biased (racial, cultural, ableist, etc.):**

**Red flags:**
- Assessor dismisses cultural context
- Assessor over-pathologizes neurodivergence
- Assessor applies different standards based on demographics
- Assessor has personal connection to case

**Response:**
1. Human Mirror flags bias immediately
2. Assessment invalidated
3. New assessor assigned (from outside region if necessary)
4. Original assessor receives feedback, may lose certification if bias confirmed

**Accountability for assessors:**
- Pattern of biased assessments → removed from assessor pool
- Single egregious bias → formal review, possible professional consequences
- Good faith errors (everyone makes mistakes) → additional training, supervision

## IX. TRAINING & CERTIFICATION

### 9.1 ASSESSOR TRAINING PROGRAM

**For licensed mental health professionals who want to conduct Flow capacity assessments:**

**Training components (20 hours total):**

**Module 1: Flow Justice Principles (4 hours)**
- How Flow differs from punitive justice
- Restoration vs. retribution
- Victim-centered but perpetrator-dignified
- Baseline inviolability

**Module 2: Capacity vs. Diagnosis (3 hours)**
- Why Flow uses capacity levels, not diagnoses
- Spectrum model (not binary)
- How capacity changes over time
- Trauma-informed perspective

**Module 3: Assessment Methodology (6 hours)**
- Interview structure
- Clinical observation
- Collateral information gathering
- Documentation standards

**Module 4: Special Populations (4 hours)**
- Children/adolescents
- Neurodivergent individuals
- Dementia/cognitive decline
- Substance use

**Module 5: Ethics & Bias (3 hours)**
- Cultural competency
- Avoiding ableism
- Recognizing own biases
- Accountability to victims AND perpetrators

**Delivered via:**
- Lyceum workshops (regional)
- Online training (accessible to rural Nodes)
- Mentorship (experienced assessors train new ones)

**Certification:**
- Complete 20 hours training
- Conduct 3 supervised assessments
- Peer review by experienced assessors
- Approved by regional mental health coordination

**Renewal:**
- Every 3 years
- 8 hours continuing education
- Peer review of recent assessments
- No pattern of bias or errors

---

### 9.2 HUMAN MIRROR TRAINING (CAPACITY SUPPORT ROLE)

**Human Mirrors who want to support capacity assessments:**

**Training components (8 hours):**

**Module 1: Basics of Capacity (2 hours)**
- What capacity means
- Three levels (Full, Partial, Impaired)
- How it affects justice response

**Module 2: Protecting Perpetrator's Rights (2 hours)**
- Privacy during assessment
- No coercive questioning
- Dignity maintained
- Trauma-informed presence

**Module 3: Supporting Assessor (2 hours)**
- What Human Mirror does during assessment (observe, not participate)
- When to interrupt (if assessment becomes punitive)
- How to provide context (Node information)

**Module 4: Cultural & Accessibility Support (2 hours)**
- Ensuring culturally appropriate communication
- Supporting neurodivergent individuals
- Language interpretation if needed

**Delivered via:**
- Lyceum local workshops
- Peer mentorship

**Certification:**
- Complete 8 hours training
- Observe 2 capacity assessments
- Conduct 1 supported role (with experienced Human Mirror)
- Approved by Circle

## XI. FINAL PRINCIPLES

### 11.1 CAPACITY AFFECTS PROCESS, NOT DIGNITY

**Person with impaired capacity:**
- Still receives Baseline
- Still has autonomy (consent to treatment)
- Still has voice (even if limited)
- Still deserves respect

**Capacity assessment is clinical evaluation, not moral judgment.**

---

### 11.2 VICTIM PROTECTION INDEPENDENT OF CAPACITY

**Even if perpetrator has impaired capacity:**
- Victim's safety is STILL paramount
- Separation maintained as long as needed
- Victim veto power STILL applies
- No pressure to forgive or reconcile

**Capacity explains harm. It does not minimize it.**

---

### 11.3 RESTORATION ADAPTED, NOT ABANDONED

**If person lacks capacity for restorative dialogue:**
- Goal shifts to: safety for victim, treatment for perpetrator, prevention of future harm
- Restoration may happen years later (if capacity improves)
- Or never (if capacity never improves)
- This is not "getting away with it" - it's honest acknowledgment that some harms cannot be restored through dialogue

**Flow adapts to reality, not fantasy.**

---

### 11.4 ASSESSMENT IS BEGINNING, NOT END

**Capacity assessment is ONE step in justice process:**
- Informs response (Level 1, 2, or 3)
- Does NOT decide outcome alone
- Victim's needs, perpetrator's needs, community safety all factor in

**Clinical data + victim voice + restorative principles = justice response.**

**Not any single element alone.**

---

## XII. CLOSING STATEMENT

**Mental health is not an excuse.**

**But it is context.**

**Flow honors both truths:**
- Harm occurred, victim deserves safety and restoration
- Perpetrator's capacity may have been impaired, treatment needed

**Traditional systems choose one:**
- Either: "Mental illness excuses everything" (dismisses victim)
- Or: "Mental illness excuses nothing" (dismisses reality of impaired capacity)

**Flow rejects false binary:**
- Impaired capacity = adapted accountability, not no accountability
- Treatment + separation + eventual restoration (if possible)
- Victim protected always, perpetrator supported (not punished)

**This is harder than binary thinking.**

**But reality is hard.**

**Flow meets reality honestly.**
