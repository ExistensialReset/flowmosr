## PURPOSE

> "Mental illness explains behavior. It does not erase harm. We hold both truths, and we act accordingly."

**The Challenge:**

When someone causes harm AND has mental illness or cognitive disability, Flow's systems intersect:

**Justice System says:**
- Harm occurred
- Accountability needed
- Restorative process
- Possibly sanctions

**Accessibility/Mental Health System says:**
- Person is disabled (mental illness = disability)
- Needs support, not punishment
- Refugium Anima access
- Accommodation required

**Both are true. Both matter. This protocol integrates them.**

---

## CORE PRINCIPLES

### 1. Dual Reality (Two Truths Coexist)

**Truth 1: Harm is Real**
- Someone was hurt
- Pain is valid
- Impact matters
- Victim deserves support and safety

**Truth 2: Suffering is Real**
- Person with mental illness is struggling
- Disability is real
- Support is needed
- Humanity remains

**The system NEVER chooses one truth over the other.**

**We hold both.**

---

### 2. Explanation ≠ Excuse

**Explanation:**
"I did this because I was in psychosis. I had minimal control."

**This is valid information.** It helps us understand what happened.

**BUT it is NOT:**
"Therefore the harm doesn't count."
"Therefore no accountability needed."
"Therefore victim should just understand."

**Mental illness EXPLAINS. It does not EXCUSE.**

**Accountability can coexist with compassion.**

---

### 3. Safety Priority (Non-Negotiable)

> **Community and victim safety override all other considerations when they conflict with ongoing support.**

**If someone repeatedly causes harm:**
- Support continues (Baseline, mental health care)
- BUT separation may be necessary
- Community safety is paramount

**This is not cruelty. This is boundary enforcement.**

---

### 4. Control is a Spectrum

**Control during harm exists on a spectrum:**

**No Control:**
- Psychosis (complete disconnection from reality)
- Severe dissociation (no awareness)
- Complete loss of agency

**Partial Control:**
- Aware but overwhelmed (depression, anxiety, PTSD)
- Impulse control reduced but not absent
- Could have chosen differently, but very difficult

**Full Control:**
- Aware of actions
- Able to choose
- Mental illness present but not removing agency

**Assessment of control affects response, but does NOT erase harm.**

---

### 5. Pattern Matters

**One incident ≠ Pattern**

Someone with mental illness who causes harm once while in crisis:
- Accountability + intensive support
- Safety planning
- Reintegration possible

**Repeated pattern:**
Someone who repeatedly causes harm despite support:
- Full accountability PLUS
- Long-term separation if pattern continues
- Community safety takes priority

**The difference is critical.**

---

### 6. Victim-Centered Always

**Regardless of perpetrator's mental illness:**

**Victim has the right to:**
- Safety (no contact if desired)
- Support (Refugium Anima if traumatized)
- Voice in process
- Decide on reconciliation (or not)
- NOT forgive
- NOT "understand" the mental illness

**Mental illness context may help victim understand "it wasn't personal," but NEVER obligates forgiveness.**

---

### 7. Non-Punitive Framework

**Flow does not punish mental illness.**

**All responses are:**
- **Protective** (keep people safe)
- **Restorative** (repair harm where possible)
- **Containment-based** (separate when necessary, but humanely)

**We do not:**
- Torture
- Dehumanize
- Deny Baseline
- Abandon

**Even in exile, even for Unforgivable Harm, even with severe mental illness:**
- Baseline guaranteed
- Mental health support continues
- Dignity maintained

## INTEGRATED RESPONSE PROCESS (IRP)

### When IRP Activates:

**Trigger conditions:**
- Harm reported
- AND mental illness/disability is relevant context

**IRP Team (Temporary Formation):**
- Mental health specialist (Refugium Anima)
- Justice facilitator (restorative justice trained)
- Victim advocate (represents harmed person's interests)
- Community observer (neutral, ensures fairness)

**NOT permanent roles. Selected per case.**

---

## STEP 1: ASSESSMENT (48-72 hours)

### Three Dimensions Assessed:

**A. Harm Level**

Using standard Flow harm categories:
- **Level 1: Friction** (minor conflict, no lasting impact)
- **Level 2: Disruption** (moderate harm, repairable)
- **Level 3: Serious Harm** (significant psychological/physical injury)
- **Level 4: Unforgivable Harm** (violence, sexual assault, child abuse, deliberate sabotage)

**B. Control Capacity During Harm**

**No Control (Psychosis/Severe Dissociation):**
- Complete disconnection from reality
- Hallucinations/delusions driving behavior
- No awareness of actions in the moment
- Cannot recall event clearly

**Indicators:**
- Diagnosed psychotic episode
- Witness accounts of complete disorientation
- No memory of event
- Behavior completely out of character

**Partial Control (Mental Illness with Awareness):**
- Aware of actions
- Reduced impulse regulation (trauma response, emotional dysregulation)
- Could have chosen differently but extremely difficult
- Mental illness made control harder but not impossible

**Indicators:**
- Depression, anxiety, PTSD, personality disorders
- Person remembers event
- Expressed conflict ("I knew it was wrong but couldn't stop")
- Some ability to de-escalate if intervention attempted

**Full Control (Mental Illness Present but Not Removing Agency):**
- Aware of actions
- Able to choose
- Mental illness may have contributed to stress/context but didn't remove control

**Indicators:**
- Clear intentionality
- Planned actions
- Able to stop when consequences threatened
- Mental illness as background, not acute crisis

**C. Pattern Recognition**

**One-Time Event:**
- First occurrence
- Clear precipitating crisis
- Out of character
- Person seeks help after

**Emerging Pattern:**
- 2-3 incidents over time
- Similar circumstances
- Escalating or repeated despite intervention
- Warning signs present

**Established Pattern:**
- 4+ incidents
- Persistent despite support
- Predictable triggers ignored
- System manipulation present

## STEP 2: RESPONSE DETERMINATION

### RESPONSE MATRIX:

| Harm Level | Control | Pattern | Response |
|------------|---------|---------|----------|
| Friction | Any | Any | Circle-level mediation + support |
| Disruption | No control | One-time | Support intensive, minimal accountability |
| Disruption | Partial/Full | One-time | Accountability + support (dual track) |
| Disruption | Any | Pattern | Escalated accountability + intensive support |
| Serious | No control | One-time | No moral accountability, mandatory treatment, safety planning |
| Serious | Partial | One-time | Full accountability + intensive support |
| Serious | Full | One-time | Full accountability, support secondary |
| Serious | Any | Pattern | Protective separation + ongoing support |
| Unforgivable | Any | Any | Exile + mental health support, victim prioritized |

## RESPONSE PROTOCOLS

### RESPONSE 1: Support Only (No Accountability Process)

**When:**
- No harm to others (person struggling, but not harming)
- OR Friction-level + genuine accident

**Protocol:**
- Refugium Anima access
- Reduced community expectations
- Monitor for escalation
- No formal accountability process

**Example:**
> Person with depression misses work commitments repeatedly
> → Support, not sanctions
> → Accommodations, not accountability

---

### RESPONSE 2: Dual Track (Accountability + Support)

**When:**
- Disruption or Serious harm
- Partial or Full control
- One-time or emerging pattern

**Protocol:**

**Accountability Track:**
1. Acknowledge harm (when stable)
2. Hear victim impact
3. Apologize (genuine, not excuse-making)
4. Repair actions agreed upon
5. Follow-through monitored

**Support Track:**
1. Crisis stabilization (Refugium Anima if needed)
2. Root cause treatment (therapy, medication, skills)
3. Safety planning (identify triggers, develop coping)
4. Long-term support (ongoing mental health care)

**Both tracks run SIMULTANEOUSLY.**

**Example: Alex (Depression + PTSD) Verbally Attacks Jordan**

```
INTEGRATED RESPONSE PLAN
─────────────────────────
​INCIDENT:
Alex (diagnosed depression + PTSD) verbally attacked Jordan during
Circle meeting, causing significant psychological distress.
​ASSESSMENT:
├─ Harm Level: Serious (psychological trauma)
├─ Control: Partial (PTSD trigger reduced impulse control, but Alex aware)
├─ Pattern: One-time (first incident of this severity)
└─ Risk: Moderate (if untreated, could recur)
​VICTIM NEEDS (Jordan):
├─ Safety: No contact with Alex during Alex's crisis period (minimum 2 weeks)
├─ Support: Refugium Anima for processing trauma from incident
├─ Apology: When Alex is stable (Jordan decides if/when to accept)
├─ Assurance: Alex is getting help, community takes this seriously
└─ Voice: Jordan participates in plan development, can opt out of reconciliation
​ACCOUNTABILITY TRACK (Alex):
├─ Acknowledge harm: "I hurt Jordan. That's real regardless of my PTSD."
├─ Apology: Written first (week 2), verbal if Jordan agrees (week 4+)
├─ Repair:
│   ├─ Respect no-contact boundary
│   ├─ Commit to treatment (demonstrated, not just promised)
│   ├─ Circle presentation on PTSD triggers (education for community)
│   └─ 6-month check-ins with Circle
└─ Consequences if not followed: Escalation to protective separation
​SUPPORT TRACK (Alex):
├─ Immediate (Week 1-2):
│   ├─ Crisis stabilization (Refugium Anima)
│   ├─ Safety assessment (self-harm risk? harm to others risk?)
│   └─ Temporary reduced community responsibilities
├─ Short-term (Week 2-12):
│   ├─ Intensive trauma therapy (EMDR, somatic work, etc.)
│   ├─ Anger/conflict de-escalation training
│   ├─ Medication review (if applicable)
│   └─ Trigger identification + coping skills development
├─ Long-term (3-12 months):
│   ├─ Ongoing therapy (weekly → biweekly → monthly)
│   ├─ Support group for PTSD (peer support)
│   ├─ Reintegration to full community participation
│   └─ Annual mental health check-ins
└─ Support continues even if accountability track fails
​COMMUNITY MEASURES:
├─ Education: Workshop on PTSD, trauma responses, how to support
├─ Protocol: Better conflict de-escalation protocols in Circle meetings
├─ Monitoring: Circle facilitator checks in with both Alex and Jordan monthly
└─ Culture shift: Normalize mental health discussions, reduce stigma
​TIMELINE:
├─ Week 1-2: Crisis stabilization, victim support, safety
├─ Week 2-4: Apology phase (when Alex stable), continued support
├─ Month 2-6: Intensive treatment, accountability check-ins
├─ Month 6-12: Reintegration, long-term support, potential reconciliation (if Jordan willing)
└─ Year 1+: Ongoing support, annual reviews, pattern monitoring
​SUCCESS INDICATORS:
├─ Jordan feels safe and supported
├─ Alex engaged with treatment (attendance, progress)
├─ No repeated incidents
├─ Community educated and prepared
└─ Relationship repair (optional, Jordan's choice)
​FAILURE INDICATORS:
├─ Alex refuses treatment
├─ Repeated harm (to Jordan or others)
├─ Jordan feels unsafe
├─ Pattern emerging
└─ → Escalate to protective separation if failure occurs

```

**Result:** Harm acknowledged, victim supported, person with mental illness gets treatment, community safer.

### RESPONSE 3: No Moral Accountability (Psychosis/No Control)

**When:**
- Serious harm
- No control (psychosis, severe dissociation)
- One-time event

**Protocol:**

**NOT ACCOUNTABLE FOR:**
- Actions during psychotic episode
- Harm caused while completely disconnected from reality

**BUT STILL REQUIRED:**
- Engage with treatment (NOT optional)
- Develop safety plan to prevent recurrence
- Possible temporary separation until stable

**VICTIM STILL SUPPORTED:**
- Harm is real even if perpetrator not responsible
- Victim gets full support (Refugium Anima, safety, etc.)
- Repair may not be possible (person doesn't remember, victim doesn't want contact)

**Example: Sam (Psychotic Episode) Attacks Stranger**

```
INCIDENT:
Sam (diagnosed schizophrenia, medication non-compliance) experienced
acute psychotic episode, believed stranger was demon, attacked them.
​ASSESSMENT:
├─ Harm Level: Serious (physical injury)
├─ Control: None (complete psychosis, no reality contact)
├─ Pattern: First violent episode (previous episodes non-violent)
└─ Risk: High if untreated, moderate if treated
​DETERMINATION:
├─ NO MORAL ACCOUNTABILITY (Sam had no control during psychosis)
├─ BUT: Mandatory treatment engagement
├─ Temporary protective separation until stable
└─ Victim support prioritized
​VICTIM SUPPORT:
├─ Medical care for injuries
├─ Refugium Anima for trauma
├─ No contact with Sam (permanent if victim chooses)
├─ Compensation from community resources (medical costs, lost work time)
└─ No pressure to "understand" or forgive
​SAM'S TREATMENT (MANDATORY):
├─ Immediate hospitalization (medical, not punitive)
├─ Medication restart + compliance monitoring
├─ Weekly psychiatric check-ins (minimum 6 months)
├─ Case management (ensure medication access, appointment attendance)
├─ Long-term: Ongoing mental health support, medication management
└─ If refuses treatment → protective separation continues
​SAFETY PLANNING:
├─ Early warning signs identified (sleep loss, paranoia, medication skip)
├─ Support Circle assigned (3-5 people check in weekly)
├─ Crisis contact list (who to call if symptoms return)
├─ Medication reminder system
└─ Annual psychiatric review
​COMMUNITY MEASURES:
├─ Education on psychosis (not dangerous most of the time, but crisis requires response)
├─ De-escalation training (how to respond if someone experiencing psychosis)
├─ Protocol: Mental health crisis response team (trained community members)
└─ Reduce stigma while maintaining safety
​REINTEGRATION (when stable):
├─ Gradual return to community spaces (monitored)
├─ Continued treatment requirement
├─ Annual mental health reviews
├─ Community acceptance not automatic (trust must be rebuilt through demonstrated stability)
└─ If victim present, strict no-contact maintained
​FAILURE PATHWAY:
├─ If Sam refuses treatment → Long-term protective separation
├─ If repeated violent psychotic episodes → Permanent separation (with continued Baseline + care)
└─ Community safety overrides Sam's preference for autonomy if pattern establishes
```

**Key principle:** Psychosis removed control → no moral blame. BUT safety planning and treatment are NOT optional.

### RESPONSE 4: Protective Separation (Pattern of Harm)

**When:**
- Repeated harm despite support
- Pattern established
- Community safety threatened

**Protocol:**

**Protective Separation IS:**
- Safety measure, not punishment
- Time-limited if possible, permanent if necessary
- With continued Baseline and mental health support
- Focused on preventing further harm

**Protective Separation is NOT:**
- Exile (unless Unforgivable Harm)
- Denial of Baseline
- Abandonment
- Torture

**Forms:**
- **Temporary no-contact** (specific people)
- **Circle exclusion** (can't attend meetings where victim present)
- **Node separation** (move to different Node, supported)
- **Regional separation** (different geographic area, Baseline guaranteed)
- **Permanent separation** (last resort, for established dangerous pattern)

**Example: Taylor (BPD) Repeated Harm Pattern**
```

INCIDENT HISTORY:
Taylor (diagnosed Borderline Personality Disorder) has caused harm 5 times in 2 years:
├─ Incident 1: Intense verbal attack on partner (mediation, therapy started)
├─ Incident 2: Threatening behavior toward Circle member (accountability + DBT therapy)
├─ Incident 3: Physical altercation (escalated, intensive therapy, temporary separation)
├─ Incident 4: Harassing messages to ex-partner (violated no-contact, sanctions)
├─ Incident 5: Vandalism of person's home (current incident)
​ASSESSMENT:
├─ Harm Level: Serious (escalating pattern, property destruction, intimidation)
├─ Control: Full (Taylor aware, BPD reduces regulation but doesn't remove control)
├─ Pattern: ESTABLISHED (5 incidents, escalating severity)
├─ Risk: HIGH (pattern persists despite multiple interventions)
​PREVIOUS INTERVENTIONS (all tried):
├─ Mediation (multiple times)
├─ Dialectical Behavior Therapy (6 months, limited progress)
├─ Medication trials (some improvement, inconsistent compliance)
├─ Accountability circles (multiple)
├─ Temporary separations (returned, harm resumed)
└─ Mental health support continuously provided
​VICTIM IMPACT:
├─ 3 different people harmed repeatedly
├─ Community members report fear
├─ Avoidance of Taylor disrupting community function
└─ Requests for Taylor's removal
​DETERMINATION: PROTECTIVE SEPARATION (Long-term)
​SEPARATION PLAN:
├─ Taylor relocates to different Node (minimum 100km away)
├─ No contact with previous victims (permanent)
├─ Cannot return to original Node without:
│   ├─ 2 years demonstrated stability
│   ├─ Successful completion of intensive therapy program
│   ├─ No incidents at new Node
│   ├─ Unanimous LOTUS approval (original Node)
│   └─ Victims consulted (can veto return even if otherwise qualified)
└─ If pattern continues at new Node → Regional separation
​TAYLOR'S SUPPORT (continues despite separation):
├─ Baseline guaranteed at new Node (housing, food, healthcare)
├─ Mental health support continues (therapy, medication, case management)
├─ Help with relocation logistics (moving, integration into new Node)
├─ No punishment, just boundary enforcement
└─ Opportunity to rebuild life in new context
​COMMUNITY MEASURES (original Node):
├─ Victim support (ongoing, all three people)
├─ Community debrief (what happened, how to prevent, how to heal)
├─ Protocol review (were early interventions adequate? what to do better?)
└─ Monitoring of similar patterns in others (early intervention)
​ACCOUNTABILITY:
├─ Taylor must acknowledge pattern (not forced, but necessary for return consideration)
├─ Must demonstrate change (actions, not words)
├─ Must respect boundaries (no contact with victims)
└─ If violated → permanent separation becomes more likely
​LONG-TERM:
├─ Annual review (how is Taylor doing at new Node?)
├─ 2-year minimum before return consideration
├─ Return NOT guaranteed (depends on demonstrated change + victim input + community safety)
└─ If pattern never changes → separation is permanent (but support continues)
```

**Principle:** We don't abandon Taylor. We don't let Taylor keep hurting people. Both can be true.

### RESPONSE 5: Exile (Unforgivable Harm)

**When:**
- Unforgivable Harm (violence, sexual assault, child abuse, deliberate Baseline sabotage)
- Regardless of mental illness

**Protocol:**

**Mental illness does NOT override community safety for Unforgivable Harm.**

**Exile process:**
1. Investigation (same as standard Unforgivable Harm protocol)
2. LOTUS tribunal (evidence review)
3. Decision: Exile or not
4. If exile: Permanent removal from Node

**WITH mental illness context:**
- Exile still happens (safety paramount)
- Mental health support continues in exile (Baseline includes mental healthcare)
- Exile location chosen with mental health access in mind
- Humanity maintained (no torture, Baseline guaranteed)

**Victim support prioritized:**
- Safety
- Healing (Refugium Anima)
- No contact ever
- Voice in process

**Example: Riley (Schizophrenia) Sexual Assault**

```
INCIDENT:
Riley (diagnosed schizophrenia, paranoid type) sexually assaulted community member.
​MENTAL ILLNESS CONTEXT:
├─ Riley has history of paranoid delusions
├─ Was experiencing active psychosis during incident (believed victim was willing participant due to delusional thinking)
├─ No previous history of sexual violence
└─ Riley's family argues "it was the psychosis, not Riley"
​ASSESSMENT:
├─ Harm Level: UNFORGIVABLE (sexual assault)
├─ Control: Debatable (psychosis present, but sexual assault indicates some directed behavior)
├─ Pattern: First incident of this type
└─ Risk: Cannot be predicted with certainty
​LOTUS TRIBUNAL DECISION:
├─ Evidence: Clear (medical examination, witnesses, Riley's partial admission)
├─ Deliberation: Mental illness considered, but Unforgivable Harm threshold met
├─ Decision: EXILE (permanent removal from Node)
└─ Vote: 78% approval (20% argued for protective separation instead, 2% argued for support only)
​REASONING PUBLISHED:
"Riley's psychosis may have distorted reality perception, but sexual assault
is Unforgivable Harm regardless of mental state. We cannot guarantee
community safety, especially victim's safety. Exile is necessary.
​We acknowledge Riley is suffering from severe mental illness. We are not
abandoning Riley. Mental health support will continue in exile. But the
victim's safety and community safety are paramount."
​EXILE IMPLEMENTATION:
├─ Riley relocated to Mammon system OR isolated Flow location (if available)
├─ Baseline provided (including psychiatric care, medication, housing)
├─ No contact with victim (permanent)
├─ Cannot enter ANY Flow Node without unanimous agreement of ALL Nodes (effectively permanent)
└─ Exile is humane but absolute
​VICTIM SUPPORT:
├─ Refugium Anima (trauma therapy, specialized sexual assault support)
├─ Community care (practical support, emotional support)
├─ Safety absolute (Riley cannot return)
├─ Justice process was victim-centered
└─ Ongoing support for as long as needed
​RILEY'S SUPPORT IN EXILE:
├─ Psychiatric care (medication management, therapy for psychosis)
├─ Baseline (food, shelter, healthcare)
├─ Humanity maintained (not tortured, not dehumanized)
├─ BUT: Exile is permanent
└─ Support does not mean return
​COMMUNITY REFLECTION:
├─ Was there earlier warning signs we missed?
├─ Could this have been prevented?
├─ How do we support people with psychosis without assuming danger?
├─ Protocol: Better mental health crisis intervention
└─ Balance: Not all people with schizophrenia are dangerous, but when Unforgivable Harm occurs, exile is necessary
```

**Principle:** Mental illness does not excuse Unforgivable Harm. Support continues, but exile is necessary.

## SPECIAL CASES

### Children and Youth (Under 18)

**Principle:** Developing brains + mental illness = different accountability framework

**Key differences:**
- **Under 13:** No formal accountability, all support
- **13-17:** Reduced accountability, heavy support emphasis
- **Pattern recognition slower** (behavior changes rapidly in adolescence)
- **Family involvement** (parents/caregivers part of plan)
- **Developmental context** (trauma, neglect, neurodevelopmental issues considered)

**Example: 15-year-old with ADHD + Conduct Disorder causes harm**

Response:
- Acknowledge harm (developmentally appropriate)
- Intensive family therapy + individual therapy
- School/Lyceum involvement
- Medication assessment
- NO exile unless extreme pattern + danger
- Long-term support (brain still developing, change very possible)

---

### Cognitive/Intellectual Disability

**Principle:** Capacity for understanding varies

**Severe cognitive disability:**
- May not understand harm caused
- Accountability reduced or absent
- Support + caregiver responsibility
- Focus on prevention, environmental modification

**Mild-moderate cognitive disability:**
- Can understand harm in concrete terms
- Simplified accountability process
- Heavy support emphasis
- Reintegration focus

---

### Addiction as Mental Illness

**Principle:** Addiction is mental illness, but harm caused while intoxicated still requires accountability

**Response:**
- Addiction treatment part of accountability
- "Being drunk/high doesn't erase harm"
- Support (Refugium Anima addiction programs)
- If pattern continues despite treatment → protective separation

---

### Personality Disorders

**Principle:** Difficult patterns but awareness present, can learn, can change (with support)

**BPD, NPD, ASPD, etc.:**
- Full accountability (awareness present, even if emotional regulation difficult)
- Specialized therapy (DBT for BPD, schema therapy for NPD, etc.)
- Longer timelines (personality change is slow)
- Pattern monitoring (if harm persists despite treatment, protective separation)

---

### Dementia / Cognitive Decline

**Principle:** Progressive loss of control requires progressive support increase

**Early stages:**
- Partial accountability (some awareness)
- Safety planning
- Caregiver support

**Late stages:**
- No accountability (no control)
- Full care + protection
- If violent behavior → specialized care environment (not punishment, just capacity-appropriate care)

## INTEGRATION WITH OTHER SYSTEMS

### With Justice System
- IRP adds mental health lens to harm assessment
- Justice facilitator part of IRP team
- Restorative justice processes adapted for mental illness context
- Sanctions possible but always paired with support

### With Accessibility System
- Mental illness = disability (Baseline guaranteed)
- Refugium Anima access
- Accommodations in accountability process (more time, communication adjustments, etc.)
- No punishment for being disabled

### With Refugium Anima
- 3-stage recovery model applies
- Crisis intervention immediate
- Long-term support guaranteed
- Participation voluntary UNLESS court-mandated as part of accountability

### With LOTUS
- IRP decisions reviewed by LOTUS if contested
- Community has voice in protective separation decisions
- Exile decisions follow standard LOTUS Tribunal process
- Mental health expertise weighted in LOTUS panels for these decisions

## FAILURE MODES & RESPONSES

### 1. Harm Excused via Mental Illness Diagnosis

**Risk:** "They have depression, so we can't hold them accountable"

**Indicators:**
- Harm minimized
- Victim sidelined
- "But they're struggling" used to avoid accountability

**Response:**
1. **Enforce dual reality:** Harm is real AND suffering is real
2. **Victim voice amplified:** What does victim need?
3. **Education:** Mental illness explains, doesn't excuse
4. **IRP team intervention:** Structured assessment prevents minimization

---

### 2. Over-Punishment Despite Mental Illness

**Risk:** Mental illness ignored, punitive response enacted

**Indicators:**
- Support denied
- Harsh sanctions
- Dehumanization
- Exile for non-Unforgivable harm

**Response:**
1. **IRP mandatory:** Can't skip to punishment without assessment
2. **Mental health advocate present:** Ensures support considered
3. **Community education:** Mental illness = disability, deserves accommodation
4. **LOTUS review:** Punitive decisions can be appealed

---

### 3. Endless Support Without Accountability

**Risk:** Support provided indefinitely despite repeated harm

**Indicators:**
- Pattern of harm continues
- Victims accumulating
- Community afraid to act
- "Give them another chance" repeatedly

**Response:**
1. **Pattern recognition enforced:** After 3-4 incidents, protective separation considered
2. **Victim safety priority:** Community safety overrides more support attempts
3. **Time limits on intervention attempts:** If no change after X months, escalate
4. **LOTUS decision:** Community decides when enough is enough

---

### 4. Victim Pressured to Forgive/Understand

**Risk:** "You should understand, they have mental illness"

**Indicators:**
- Victim feels guilty for not forgiving
- Community minimizes victim's trauma
- Reconciliation pushed before victim ready

**Response:**
1. **Victim autonomy absolute:** Forgiveness NEVER required
2. **Victim advocate enforces boundaries:** Protects victim from pressure
3. **Education:** Understanding context ≠ obligation to forgive
4. **No-contact respected:** Even if perpetrator "better now," victim decides contact

---

### 5. Mental Illness Faked/Exaggerated

**Risk:** Person claims mental illness to avoid accountability

**Indicators:**
- Symptoms inconsistent
- Only present when convenient
- Mental health professionals skeptical
- Pattern of manipulation

**Response:**
1. **Professional assessment:** Mental health specialist evaluates
2. **Pattern analysis:** Does behavior match diagnosis?
3. **Multiple sources:** Don't rely on self-report alone
4. **If faking detected:** Treated as manipulation (serious harm), full accountability

---

### 6. Reintegration Too Fast

**Risk:** Person returns before ready, harms again

**Indicators:**
- Minimal time in treatment
- No demonstrated stability
- Victim concerns ignored
- Community pressure to "give them chance"

**Response:**
1. **Mandatory timelines:** Minimum treatment duration before reintegration
2. **Stability demonstrated:** Not just claimed, but evidenced over time
3. **Victim input required:** Victim can veto premature return
4. **Gradual reintegration:** Phased return, not all-at-once

## ASSESSMENT QUESTIONS

### Can Your Node Answer These?

**Integration:**
1. Do you have IRP team structure (mental health + justice + victim advocate)?
2. Are both accountability AND support provided when mental illness involved?
3. Do you distinguish between explanation and excuse clearly?
4. Is victim safety prioritized even when perpetrator has mental illness?
5. Are patterns recognized and addressed (not endless "one more chance")?

**Accountability:**
6. Can people with mental illness be held accountable appropriately?
7. Is control capacity assessed (not assumed)?
8. Do sanctions exist alongside support?
9. Is protective separation used when needed?
10. Are consequences proportional to harm, not to diagnosis?
